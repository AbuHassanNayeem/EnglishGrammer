# LEVEL 2 ‚Äî MORPHOLOGY: WORD STRUCTURE

## 2.1 Morphemes: The Basic Building Blocks

**Morpheme**: The smallest unit of meaning in a language.

### Free Morphemes
- Can stand alone as independent words
- Examples: *book*, *run*, *cat*, *happy*

### Bound Morphemes
Cannot function independently; must attach to other morphemes.

#### Inflectional Morphemes (Grammatical markers)
Modify the grammatical form without changing word class:
- **Tense**: *walk* ‚Üí *walked*
- **Number**: *cat* ‚Üí *cats*
- **Person/Agreement**: *I go* vs *She goes*
- **Comparison**: *fast* ‚Üí *faster* ‚Üí *fastest*
- **Case**: *he* ‚Üí *him*

#### Derivational Morphemes (Category-changing)
Create new words, often changing word class:
- **Noun ‚Üí Adjective**: *beauty* ‚Üí *beautiful*
- **Verb ‚Üí Noun**: *create* ‚Üí *creation*
- **Adjective ‚Üí Adverb**: *quick* ‚Üí *quickly*
- **Zero Derivation**: *a gift* ‚Üí *to gift* (no morpheme added)

---

## 2.2 Word Formation Processes

### Affixation (Prefixation / Suffixation)
- Adding prefixes or suffixes to a root word
- Examples: *un-happy*, *re-write*, *care-ful*, *care-less*

### Compounding
- Joining two independent words
- **Spaced/Open**: *bus stop*, *post office*, *tea party*
- **Hyphenated**: *mother-in-law*, *father-in-law*
- **Closed/Solid**: *snowball*, *keyboard*, *notebook*

### Conversion (Zero Derivation)
- Changing word class without adding morphemes
- Examples: *butter* (N) ‚Üí *to butter* (V), *run* (N) ‚Üí *to run* (V)

### Clipping
- Shortening longer words
- Examples: *advertisement* ‚Üí *ad*, *telephone* ‚Üí *phone*

### Blending
- Combining parts of two words
- Examples: *smoke* + *fog* ‚Üí *smog*, *breakfast* + *lunch* ‚Üí *brunch*

### Acronyms
- Using initial letters to form new words
- Examples: *NASA*, *SCUBA*, *RADAR*

### Back-formation
- Creating a new word by removing what seems like an affix
- Examples: *edit* (from *editor*), *burgle* (from *burglar*)

# LEVEL 3 ‚Äî SYNTAX: SENTENCE STRUCTURE

## 3.1 The Sentence

**Definition**: A complete grammatical unit expressing a full thought.

### Sentence Types (by Structure)

#### Simple Sentence
- **One independent clause** only
- Structure: Subject + Predicate
- Example: *She studies hard.*

#### Compound Sentence
- **Two or more independent clauses** joined by coordinating conjunctions
- Conjunctions: *and, but, or, yet, so, nor*
- Example: *She studied hard **and** she passed the exam.*

#### Complex Sentence
- **One independent clause + one or more dependent clauses**
- Example: *She passed the exam **because** she studied hard.*

#### Compound-Complex Sentence
- **Two or more independent clauses + one or more dependent clauses**
- Example: *She studied hard **and** she passed the exam **because** she was determined.*

---

## 3.2 Clauses: Independent vs. Dependent

### Independent (Main) Clause
- Can stand alone as a complete sentence
- Must contain at least a subject and a finite verb
- Example: *The cat is sleeping.*

### Dependent (Subordinate) Clause
- Cannot stand alone; requires an independent clause
- Always introduced by a subordinating word (conjunction, pronoun, adverb)

#### Types by Function

**Noun Clause** (Functions as noun: subject, object, complement)
- Examples:
  - Subject: *What he said is important.*
  - Object: *I know that she is coming.*
  - Complement: *The fact is that she won.*

**Adjective (Relative) Clause** (Modifies a noun)
- Example: *The book that I read was excellent.*

**Adverbial Clause** (Modifies verb, adjective, or adverb)
- Example: *She succeeded **because** she worked hard.*

---

## 3.3 Clauses: Finite vs. Non-Finite

### Finite Clause
- Contains a **finite verb** (marked for tense and agreement)
- Example: *He goes to school.* / *She was sleeping.*

### Non-Finite Clause
- Contains a **non-finite verb** (not marked for tense)

#### To-Infinitive Clause
- Form: *to + base verb*
- Example: *To succeed is important.* / *I want to go.*

#### -ing Clause (Gerund/Participle)
- Form: *verb + -ing*
- Example: *Running is healthy.* / *I saw him entering the room.*

#### -ed Clause (Past Participle)
- Form: *verb + -ed*
- Example: *Broken windows need repair.* / *Once finished, he left.*

---

## 3.4 Phrase Structure

**Definition**: A group of words without a finite verb, functioning as a single unit.

### Types of Phrases

**Noun Phrase (NP)**
- Head: Noun or pronoun
- Structure: Determiner + Modifiers + Head + Post-modifiers
- Example: *the tall building*, *a beautiful painting*

**Verb Phrase (VP)**
- Head: Verb (lexical or auxiliary)
- Structure: Auxiliary Verbs + Main Verb + Objects/Complements
- Example: *will have been studying*, *has completed the task*

**Prepositional Phrase (PP)**
- Head: Preposition
- Structure: Preposition + Noun Phrase
- Example: *in the park*, *under the table*, *with enthusiasm*

**Adjective Phrase (AdjP)**
- Head: Adjective
- Structure: Degree modifier + Adjective + (complement)
- Example: *very happy*, *quite intelligent*, *fond of music*

**Adverb Phrase (AdvP)**
- Head: Adverb
- Structure: Degree modifier + Adverb
- Example: *very quickly*, *quite happily*, *too slowly*

## 3.5 Clause Structure: The CP‚ÄìTP‚ÄìVP System

This represents the **hierarchical shell** of a clause in generative syntax.

```
CP (Complementizer Phrase) ‚Äî OUTER SHELL (Mood/Force)
‚îÇ
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ Spec-CP: Wh-phrases / Topic / Focus
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ C (Head): Complementizers (that, if, whether, √ò)
‚îÇ
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ TP (Tense Phrase) ‚Äî FINITE SHELL (Agreement)
    ‚îÇ
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ Spec-TP: SUBJECT (NP/DP)
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ T (Head): Tense (Present, Past, Modal, √ò)
    ‚îÇ
    ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ VP (Verb Phrase) ‚Äî LEXICAL SHELL (Action/Meaning)
        ‚îÇ
        ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ Spec-VP: External Argument (Agent)
        ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ V (Head): Lexical Verb
        ‚îÇ   (Action, State, Event, Process)
        ‚îÇ
        ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ Complements: Objects, PPs, Embedded Clauses
        ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ Adjuncts: Adverbials
```

### Component Breakdown

**CP (Complementizer Phrase)**
- The outermost layer controlling mood and force
- Contains the complementizer (*that*, *if*, *whether*, or null)
- Example: *[CP that [TP she arrived]]* in "I know that she arrived"

**TP (Tense Phrase)**
- The layer responsible for finiteness and tense marking
- Subject position (Spec-TP) hosts the grammatical subject
- Tense head marks temporal information
- Example: *[TP She [T' is [VP reading]]]* in "She is reading"

**VP (Verb Phrase)**
- The innermost lexical layer containing the verb and its arguments
- The verb assigns thematic roles to its arguments
- Adjuncts attach at various levels for adverbial modification

## 3.6 Sentence Elements: The Core Components

### SUBJECT (S)

**Definition**: The entity performing the action or being described.

#### Subject Realizations

**Noun Phrase**: *The old man is walking.*

**Pronoun**: *He is walking.* / *They are here.*

**Gerund Phrase**: *Swimming is good for health.*

**Infinitive Phrase**: *To wait is hard.*

**Infinitival Clause**: *For him to arrive on time is difficult.*

**That-Clause / Finite Nominal Clause**: *That he failed is sad.*

**Dummy/Expletive Subject**: *It rains.* / *There are cats.*

#### Internal NP/DP Structure

```
NP/DP
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ Determiner: the, a, this, every, some, √ò
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ Pre-modifiers: Adjectives, degree words, noun modifiers
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ HEAD: Noun or Pronoun
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ Post-modifiers: Relative clauses, prepositional phrases, non-finite clauses
```

---

### PREDICATE (P) / VERB PHRASE (VP)

**Definition**: The verb phrase expressing the action, state, or being.

#### Predicator (Verb)

**Main Verbs** (Semantic content)
- **Transitive**: Takes a direct object (*eat, buy, read*)
- **Intransitive**: Takes no object (*sleep, arrive, laugh*)
- **Ditransitive**: Takes both direct and indirect objects (*give, send, teach, show*)
- **Linking**: Connects subject to complement (*be, seem, appear*)

**Auxiliary Verbs** (Grammatical function)
- **Primary**: *be, have, do*
  - *be*: passive voice, progressive aspect
  - *have*: perfect aspect
  - *do*: negation, questions, emphasis
- **Modal**: *can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would, ought to*
  - Express ability, permission, obligation, possibility, futurity

**Verb Forms**
- **Base**: *walk, eat, run*
- **-s form**: *walks, eats, runs* (3rd person singular)
- **-ing form**: *walking, eating, running* (present participle, gerund)
- **-ed form**: *walked, ate, run* (past tense or past participle)

---

### OBJECTS (O)

**Direct Object (DO)**
- The recipient of the action; the entity affected by the action
- Follows transitive and ditransitive verbs
- Example: *She plays guitar.* / *I eat an apple.*

**Indirect Object (IO)**
- The beneficiary or recipient; typically receives the benefit
- Occurs in ditransitive verb structures
- Usually precedes the direct object in English
- Example: *I gave him a gift.* / *She sent me a message.*
- **Exam Tip**: Can often be replaced by a prepositional phrase: *I gave a gift to him.*

---

### COMPLEMENTS (C)

**Subject Complement**
- Follows a linking verb
- Identifies, describes, or renames the subject
- Can be a noun, adjective, or noun phrase
- Example: *She is a teacher.* / *He seems happy.* / *The food tastes delicious.*

**Object Complement**
- Follows the direct object
- Identifies or describes the direct object
- Completes the meaning of the verb and object
- Example: *They elected him president.* / *I painted the door blue.*

---

### ADVERBIALS (A)

**Definition**: Elements providing additional information about time, place, manner, reason, condition, etc.

#### Adjuncts (Essential Context)
Modify the verb and provide situational information:
- **Time**: *tomorrow, yesterday, at 5 o'clock*
- **Place**: *in the park, at home, under the table*
- **Manner**: *quickly, carefully, silently*
- **Reason**: *because of the rain, due to illness*
- **Condition**: *if it rains, unless you object*
- **Purpose**: *to win, in order to succeed*
- **Frequency**: *always, never, sometimes*

#### Conjuncts (Discourse Connectors)
Link sentences or clauses for coherence:
- **Addition**: *moreover, furthermore, in addition*
- **Contrast**: *however, nevertheless, on the other hand*
- **Result**: *therefore, consequently, thus*
- **Summary**: *in conclusion, to sum up*

#### Disjuncts (Speaker Stance)
Indicate the speaker's attitude or perspective:
- **Attitude**: *frankly, honestly, fortunately, unfortunately*
- **Style**: *in my opinion, it seems to me, to be honest*

## 3.7 Noun Phrase Structure: Detailed Breakdown

### Noun: The Head of the Phrase

#### Proper Nouns
- Specific names of people, places, or things
- Always capitalized
- Examples: *London, Sarah, Monday, Microsoft*

#### Common Nouns
- General names for people, places, things, or ideas

##### Concrete Nouns
- Can be perceived through senses
- Examples: *table, tree, apple, water*

###### Countable Nouns
- Can be counted; have singular and plural forms
- **Individual items**: *book, chair, pen, man, city*
- **Plural items**: *books, chairs, pens, men, cities*
- **Collective Nouns**: Groups or collections
  - Groups: *team, flock, herd, swarm, pack*
  - Collections/Institutions: *library, gallery, orchestra, choir*

###### Uncountable (Mass) Nouns
- Cannot be counted; no plural form
- Require singular verbs
- Examples: *water, air, fire, food, furniture, information*
- **Note**: Some nouns can be both countable and uncountable depending on context
  - *I ate some cheese.* (uncountable) vs *We bought three cheeses.* (countable)

##### Abstract Nouns
- Refer to ideas, qualities, emotions, states
- Examples: *love, courage, honesty, knowledge, happiness, freedom*

##### Compound Nouns
- Formed from two or more words
- **Spaced/Open**: *bus stop, post office, tea party*
- **Hyphenated**: *mother-in-law, father-in-law, son-in-law*
- **Closed/Solid**: *snowball, keyboard, notebook*

---

### Pronoun: Alternative Head

**Definition**: Words that replace or refer to nouns (antecedents).

#### Personal Pronouns

**Subjective (Nominative Case)**: *I, we, you, he, she, it, they*
- Used as subjects
- Example: *I go to school.* / *They are here.*

**Objective (Accusative Case)**: *me, us, you, him, her, it, them*
- Used as objects of verbs or prepositions
- Example: *She gave me a gift.* / *Come with us.*

**Possessive Personal Pronouns**:
- **Determiner Form** (before nouns): *my, our, your, his, her, its, their*
  - Example: *My book is here.* / *Her dress is beautiful.*
- **Absolute Form** (standalone): *mine, ours, yours, his, hers, its, theirs*
  - Example: *The book is mine.* / *These chairs are ours.*

#### Reflexive Pronouns
- Refer back to the subject
- Forms: *myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves, oneself*
- Usage: *I cut myself.* / *She saw herself in the mirror.*

#### Emphatic (Intensive) Pronouns
- Same forms as reflexive but used for emphasis
- Example: *I myself believe this.* / *The president himself attended.*
- **Difference**: Reflexive pronouns are necessary for meaning; emphatic are optional

#### Demonstrative Pronouns
- Point to specific entities
- Forms: *this, that, these, those, such, the same*
- Example: *This is mine.* / *Those are old.*

#### Interrogative Pronouns
- Used in questions
- Forms: *who, whom, whose, which, what*
- Example: *Who arrived?* / *What did you buy?*

#### Relative Pronouns
- Introduce relative clauses; relate to a noun (antecedent)
- Forms: *who, whom, whose, which, that, whoever, whomever, whichever, whatever*
- Example: *The man who arrived is my teacher.*
- **Difference from Interrogative**: Same forms but different function‚Äîrelative pronouns don't ask questions

#### Indefinite Pronouns
- Refer to non-specific or general entities

**Universal**: *all, both, each, every, everyone, everybody, everything*
- Example: *Everyone should attend.* / *All are welcome.*

#### Distributive Pronouns
- Show distribution or individuality; take singular verbs
- Forms: *each, either, neither*
- Example: *Each student received a gift.* / *Neither option is suitable.*

**Quantitative**: *some, any, much, many, few, little, several, enough, more, most*
- Example: *Some students passed.* / *Many people attended.*

**Negative**: *none, nobody, nothing, no one, neither*
- Example: *Nobody came.* / *Nothing happened.*

**Existential**: *someone, somebody, something, anyone, anybody, anything*
- Example: *Someone called me.* / *Anything is possible.*

#### Reciprocal Pronouns
- Show mutual action between entities
- Forms: *each other, one another*
- Example: *They love each other.* / *We helped one another.*

#### Dummy/Expletive Pronouns
- No referential meaning; required for grammatical structure
- Forms: *it, there*
- Usage: *It rains.* / *There are cats.* / *It is obvious.*

### Gerunds: Verbs Functioning as Nouns

**Definition**: Verb + -ing used as a noun, retaining some verb properties.

#### Core Identity
- **Form**: Base Verb + -ing (e.g., *walking, eating, being*)
- **Nature**: Verb-origin but noun-function
- **Distinction**: ‚â† Present Participle (adjectival use)
- **Usage**: Subject, object, predicate, complement, etc.

#### Gerund by Function in Sentence

**Subject**: *Swimming is good for health.* / *Smoking causes cancer.*

**Object of Verb**: *I enjoy reading.* / *He finished writing.* / *They suggested going.*

**Object of Preposition**: *He is fond of singing.* / *Tired of waiting.* / *Interested in painting.*

**Subject Complement**: *My hobby is painting.* / *Seeing is believing.*

**Appositive**: *His aim, winning the match, was achieved.* / *Her dream, becoming a doctor, came true.*

#### Gerund by Verb Association

**Verbs Followed by Gerund Only**:
*admit, avoid, appreciate, enjoy, finish, deny, consider, suggest, risk, mind, delay, practice, quit, keep, postpone, resist, tolerate*

**Verbs with Meaning Change (Gerund vs. Infinitive)**:
- **Remember**: Gerund = past action (*I remember meeting him*) | Infinitive = future duty (*Remember to meet him*)
- **Stop**: Gerund = end activity (*He stopped smoking*) | Infinitive = stop to do something (*He stopped to smoke*)
- **Try**: Gerund = experiment (*Try using this key*) | Infinitive = effort (*I tried to open the door*)
- **Regret**: Gerund = remorse for past (*I regret saying that*) | Infinitive = giving bad news (*I regret to inform you*)
- **Forget**: Gerund = past memory (*I forgot meeting her*) | Infinitive = neglecting duty (*Don't forget to meet her*)

**Verbs Followed by Infinitive Only**:
*decide, plan, promise, hope, refuse, offer, agree, deserve, fail, learn, manage*

#### Gerund vs. Present Participle

**Gerund** (Acts as noun): *Smoking is harmful.* / *I love singing.*

**Participle** (Acts as adjective/verb modifier): *The smoking man left.* / *He is singing a song.*

#### Gerund vs. Verbal Noun

**Gerund**: Takes direct object; retains verb properties (*Building the house was fast*)

**Verbal Noun**: Takes "of"; acts fully as noun (*The building of the house*)

### Infinitives: Base Verb Forms

#### Infinitive Phrase (INF-PHRASE)
- **Form**: *to + verb*

- **Example**: *To learn is important.*

#### Infinitival Clause (INF-CLAUSE)

### That-Clause vs. Finite Nominal Clause

#### That-Clause
- **Definition**: Subordinate clause introduced by "that"
- **Verb Type**: Usually finite (tense-marked)
- **Key**: "That" is explicitly present
- **Function**: Can act as noun, adjective, or adverb
- **Examples**: *I know that she is coming.* / *It is clear that he made a mistake.*

#### Finite Nominal Clause
- **Definition**: Clause functioning as a noun
- **Verb Type**: Always finite
- **Introducer**: May or may not use "that"
- **Function**: Always noun (subject, object, complement)
- **Examples**: *That she is coming is obvious.* / *I think he is honest.*

#### Key Differences

| Aspect | That-Clause | Finite Nominal Clause |
|--------|------------|----------------------|
| Introducer | Must use "that" | May or may not use "that" |
| Verb Type | Usually finite | Always finite |
| Function | Any clause type | Always functions as noun |

### Dummy/Expletive Subjects

**Definition**: Non-referential subjects required by English grammar structure.

#### Types

**Expletive It**:
- **Weather**: *It rains.* / *It is snowing.*
- **Time**: *It is 5 o'clock.* / *It is Monday.*
- **Distance**: *It is far to the station.*
- **Impersonal**: *It seems that she is right.* / *It is important to study.*

**Expletive There** (Existential):
- Introduces existence of something
- *There are cats in the garden.* / *There is a problem.*
- **Verb Agreement**: *There is* (singular) vs. *There are* (plural)

#### Exam Tips
- Check if *it* or *there* lacks real referential meaning
- With *there*, verb agrees with the noun that follows
- Dummy *it* signals impersonal statements, weather, or temporal/distance information

### Determiners

**Definition**: Words specifying reference, quantity, possession, or definiteness of nouns.

#### Types

**Articles**:
- **Definite**: *the* (specific entity)
- **Indefinite**: *a, an* (non-specific entity)
- **Exam Tip**: Use *a* before consonant sounds, *an* before vowel sounds

**Demonstratives**: *this, that, these, those*
- Indicate proximity or distance
- Example: *This book is mine.* / *Those chairs are old.*

**Quantifiers**: *some, any, every, no, few, many, much, most, all, half, both, either, neither, each*
- Express quantity or amount
- Example: *Few students passed.* / *All the cookies are gone.*

**Numbers**:
- **Cardinal**: *one, two, three‚Ä¶*
- **Ordinal**: *first, second, third‚Ä¶*

**Possessives**: *my, your, his, her, its, our, their, whose*
- Show ownership or relationship
- Example: *Her dress is beautiful.* / *Whose bag is this?*

**Interrogative Determiners**: *which, what, whose*
- Used in questions
- Example: *Which color do you prefer?*

**Distributive Determiners**: *each, every, either, neither*
- Show distribution or individuality
- Example: *Each student must submit homework.*

#### Exam Tips
- Determiners always precede adjectives and nouns: *the red car*
- Most determiners cannot be combined: *the my book* ‚ùå
- Count vs. uncount determines determiner choice: *many books*, *much water*

---

### Pre-modifiers

**Definition**: Words or phrases modifying the noun from before it.

#### Types

**Adjective Phrases**:
- Single adjectives: *tall, beautiful, intelligent*
- Adjective + modifier: *very tall, extremely happy*
- Example: *A very tall building stands here.*

**Degree Words (Intensifiers)**: *very, too, so, extremely, quite*
- Example: *She is very intelligent.* / *He is too tired.*

**Order of Multiple Pre-modifiers**:
1. Determiner ‚Üí 2. Quantifier/Number ‚Üí 3. Opinion ‚Üí 4. Size ‚Üí 5. Age ‚Üí 6. Color ‚Üí 7. Material ‚Üí 8. Purpose ‚Üí Noun
- Example: *The three large old brown wooden dining tables*

---

### Post-modifiers

**Definition**: Words or phrases modifying the noun from after it.

#### Types

**Relative Clauses**:
- Introduced by relative pronouns (*who, which, that*) or adverbs (*where, when, why*)
- **Defining/Restrictive**: Essential to meaning (*The man who arrived is my teacher.*)
- **Non-defining/Non-restrictive**: Extra information, set off by commas (*My father, who is a doctor, is here.*)

**Prepositional Phrases**:
- Express location, time, relation, etc.
- Example: *The book on the table belongs to Sarah.* / *The man with the hat is my uncle.*

**Non-finite Clauses**:
- **Present Participle**: *The man standing near the gate is my friend.*
- **Past Participle**: *The letter written by her was beautiful.*
- **Infinitive**: *He is the person to ask for help.*

**Appositive Phrases**:
- Renames or explains the noun
- Example: *My friend, a talented musician, won the competition.*

## 3.8 Pronouns in Detail

### Personal Pronoun Cases

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Subjective | I, you, he, she, it | we, you, they |
| Objective | me, you, him, her, it | us, you, them |
| Possessive | my, your, his, her, its | our, your, their |

#### Case Assignment Rules
- **Subjective Case**: Subject of finite verb (*She runs fast.*)
- **Objective Case**: Object of verb or preposition (*I saw him.* / *with me*)
- **Possessive Case**: Shows ownership (*My book is here.*)

#### Common Exam Traps
- Prepositions always take objective case: *for me* ‚úî / *for I* ‚ùå
- In coordinate structures: *She and I went home.* (both subjects) vs. *They invited him and me.* (both objects)
- Comparative ellipsis: *He is taller than I (am).* (formal) vs. *taller than me* (informal)

### Who vs. Whom
- **Who**: Subject position (*Who arrived?*)
- **Whom**: Object position (*Whom did you see?*)
- **Exam Tip**: Mentally substitute he/him (*he arrived* ‚Üí *who*; *I saw him* ‚Üí *whom*)

## 3.9 Agreement & Concord

**Definition**: Grammatical matching between sentence elements.

### Subject‚ÄìVerb Agreement
- Verb must agree with subject in person and number
- Example: *She **goes*** (3rd person singular) vs. *They **go*** (3rd person plural)
- **Exam Trap**: Intervening phrases don't affect agreement
  - *The group of students **are** here.* (subject = "group", plural collective)
  - *One of the girls **has** arrived.* (subject = "one", singular)

### Pronoun‚ÄìAntecedent Agreement
- Pronoun must agree with antecedent (noun it replaces)
- Example: *The girl said **she** was tired.* / *The boys said **they** were tired.*

## 3.10 Verb Morphology & Tense

### Verb Forms

**Base Form** (Infinitive): *walk, eat, run*

**3rd Person Singular** (-s form): *walks, eats, runs*

**Past Tense** (-ed form, or irregular): *walked, ate, ran*

**Present Participle** (-ing form): *walking, eating, running*

**Past Participle** (-ed form or irregular): *walked, eaten, run*

### The 12 Tenses

#### Present
- **Simple Present**: *I walk.* (habitual action)
- **Present Progressive**: *I am walking.* (ongoing action)
- **Present Perfect**: *I have walked.* (completed action with relevance)
- **Present Perfect Progressive**: *I have been walking.* (ongoing action from past to present)

#### Past
- **Simple Past**: *I walked.* (completed action)
- **Past Progressive**: *I was walking.* (ongoing action in past)
- **Past Perfect**: *I had walked.* (action completed before another past action)
- **Past Perfect Progressive**: *I had been walking.* (ongoing action before another past event)

#### Future
- **Simple Future**: *I will walk.* (future prediction)
- **Future Progressive**: *I will be walking.* (ongoing future action)
- **Future Perfect**: *I will have walked.* (future action completed before a point)
- **Future Perfect Progressive**: *I will have been walking.* (ongoing future action up to a point)

### Aspect
- **Perfect**: Completed action with relevance to present (auxiliary *have* + past participle)
- **Progressive**: Action in progress (auxiliary *be* + present participle)

### Voice
- **Active Voice**: Subject performs the action (*The cat ate the fish.*)
- **Passive Voice**: Subject receives the action (*The fish was eaten by the cat.*)

### Mood
- **Indicative**: Statement of fact (*She is here.*)
- **Imperative**: Command (*Come here.*)
- **Subjunctive**: Hypothetical or contrary to fact (*If I were you‚Ä¶*)

---

## 3.11 Transitive, Intransitive, and Ditransitive Verbs

### Transitive Verbs
- Require a direct object
- Example: *She plays **guitar**.* / *I ate **an apple**.*

### Intransitive Verbs
- Do not take an object
- Example: *She **arrived**.* / *The baby **cried**.*

### Ditransitive Verbs
- Take both direct and indirect objects
- Example: *I gave **him** **a gift**.* / *She sent **me** **a message**.*

### Linking Verbs
- Connect subject to subject complement
- Common: *be, become, seem, appear, remain, look, taste, smell, sound, feel*
- Example: *She is **a teacher**.* / *He seems **happy**.*

---

## 3.12 Word Order & Inversion

### Basic Pattern: SVO
English uses **Subject-Verb-Object** word order.
- Example: *The boy **hit** the ball.*

### Variations

#### Inversion (V-S-O)
- Reversal of normal word order
- **Questions**: *Does she eat fruit?*
- **Emphasis**: *Never have I seen such beauty.*
- **After negation**: *Not only did he fail, but he also quit.*

#### Passive Voice
- Changes focus: O becomes subject, S becomes agent
- Active: *The teacher taught the lesson.*
- Passive: *The lesson was taught by the teacher.*

#### Fronting / Extraposition
- **Fronting**: Moving elements to sentence-initial position
  - *The money, he didn't find.*
- **Extraposition**: Moving elements to sentence-final position
  - *It is clear [that she is brilliant].*

---

## 3.13 Coordination & Subordination

### Coordination
- Joining equal grammatical units
- **Conjunctions**: *and, but, or, yet, so, nor*
- **Structure**: Independent clause + Conjunction + Independent clause
- **Exam Focus**: Maintain parallel structure
  - ‚úì *She reads **and** writes* effectively.
  - ‚úó *She reads **and** writing* effectively.

### Subordination
- Joining unequal units (main + dependent clause)
- **Conjunctions**: *because, although, if, that, when, where, why, which, who*
- **Structure**: Main clause + Subordinating conjunction + Dependent clause
- Example: *She passed the exam **because** she studied hard.*

---

## 3.14 Information Structure

### Theme‚ÄìRheme (Topic‚ÄìComment)

**Theme (Topic)**: The starting point; what the sentence is about
- Typically subject position
- Example: *She worked hard.* (Theme = "She")

**Rheme (Comment)**: The new information about the theme
- Example: *She worked hard.* (Rheme = "worked hard")

### Given vs. New Information

**Given**: Information already known from context
- Example: *The cat is sleeping. **The cat** (given) is old.* (new = old)

**New**: Information introduced for the first time
- Example: *What is the cat doing? The cat is **sleeping.*** (new = sleeping)

### Focus & Emphasis

**Focus**: The part carrying the most important or new information
- Example: *She didn't eat the cake.* (neutral) vs. *She ate the **cake**, not the pie.* (focused on "cake")

**Emphasis**: Highlighting elements through stress, intonation, or word order
- **Cleft Construction**: *It was **him** who told me.* (emphasis on "him")

# LEVEL 4 ‚Äî THE DETERMINER PHRASE (DP Hypothesis)

Modern generative syntax treats noun phrases as **Determiner Phrases** (DP), not simple NPs.

## 4.1 DP Structure

```

‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ D (Determiner Head): the, a, this, every, some, √ò

    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ Spec-NP: Modifiers (e.g., very tall)

    ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ Complement/Modifier: in the park, who I met
```

## 4.2 D-Selection
The determiner governs what type of noun or noun phrase can follow:

- **Definite articles** (*the*): Can precede both count and mass nouns
  - *the book, the water*
- **Indefinite articles** (*a, an*): Only precede count nouns
  - *a book* ‚úî / *a water* ‚úó
- **Demonstratives** (*this, that*): Both count and mass
  - *this book, that water*
- **Quantifiers** (*some, any, many*): Depend on count/mass
  - *many books* ‚úî / *many water* ‚úó

# LEVEL 5 ‚Äî ARGUMENT STRUCTURE & VALENCY

## 5.1 Valency: How Many Arguments?

**Valency** describes the number of obligatory participants a verb requires.

### Avalent (0 arguments)
- Predicate takes no arguments
- Example: *It rains.* / *It snows.*

### Monovalent (1 argument)
- Requires only a subject
- Example: *She **sleeps**.* / *The baby **cried**.*

### Bivalent (2 arguments)
- Requires subject and object
- Example: *He **eats** an apple.* / *She **likes** music.*

### Trivalent (3 arguments)
- Requires subject, direct object, and indirect object
- Example: *She **gave** him a gift.* / *I **showed** them the pictures.*

---

## 5.2 Thematic Roles (Œ∏-Roles)

**Thematic roles** describe the semantic function of arguments in a sentence.

### Core Roles

**Agent**: The entity performing or controlling the action
- Example: *The boy* hit the ball.

**Patient/Theme**: The entity directly affected by the action
- Example: The boy hit *the ball.*

**Experiencer**: The entity experiencing a state or perception
- Example: *I* feel cold.

**Beneficiary**: The entity receiving benefit from an action
- Example: She baked a cake *for him.*

### Peripheral Roles

**Instrument**: The tool or means used to perform an action
- Example: He opened the door *with a key.*

**Location**: Where the action takes place
- Example: She works *in the park.*

**Source**: Where something originates
- Example: He came *from London.*

**Goal**: The destination or target
- Example: She went *to the store.*

---

## 5.3 Mapping Between Syntax and Semantics

Thematic roles typically map to syntactic positions:

| Thematic Role | Typical Syntactic Position |
|---------------|---------------------------|
| Agent | Subject |
| Experiencer | Subject |
| Theme/Patient | Direct Object |
| Recipient/Beneficiary | Indirect Object |
| Instrument | Object of "with" |
| Location | Adverbial (PP) |

# LEVEL 6 ‚Äî FEATURES & AGREEMENT

## 6.1 Feature System

### Nominal Features (Phi-features)

**Person**: 1st (*I, we*), 2nd (*you*), 3rd (*he, she, it, they*)

**Number**: Singular vs. Plural
- *The cat is sleeping.* (singular) vs. *The cats are sleeping.* (plural)

**Gender**: Masculine, Feminine, Neuter (visible mainly in pronouns)
- *he* (masculine), *she* (feminine), *it* (neuter)

**Case**: Subjective, Objective, Possessive (remnants in English)
- *I* (subj), *me* (obj), *my* (poss)

### Verbal Features

**Tense**: Present, Past, Future (marked by auxiliary or main verb form)

**Aspect**: Perfect (*have* + pp), Progressive (*be* + ing)

**Mood**: Indicative, Imperative, Subjunctive

**Voice**: Active (*The cat ate the fish*) vs. Passive (*The fish was eaten by the cat*)

---

## 6.2 Agreement Rules

### Subject‚ÄìVerb Agreement (in English)
- **Present tense, 3rd person singular adds -s**
  - *He **walks**.* vs. *They **walk**.*
- **Irregular verbs** (e.g., *be*)
  - *I **am**, you **are**, he **is**, we **are**, they **are***

### Pronoun‚ÄìAntecedent Agreement
- Pronoun must match antecedent in person and number
- Gender agreement is traditional but evolving (singular "they" is increasingly used for non-binary or unknown gender)
- Example: *The teacher said **they** would help.* / *The teacher said **she** would help.* (teacher = antecedent)

# LEVEL 7 ‚Äî SEMANTICS: MEANING

## 7.1 Lexical Meaning

**Lexical meaning**: The inherent meaning of individual words.

### Sense Relations

**Synonymy**: Words with similar meanings
- *big* ‚Üî *large*, *happy* ‚Üî *joyful*

**Antonymy**: Words with opposite meanings
- *big* ‚Üî *small*, *hot* ‚Üî *cold*

**Hyponymy**: Hierarchical relationship (specific to general)
- *dog* is a hyponym of *animal*

**Polysemy**: One word with multiple related meanings
- *bank* (financial institution vs. side of a river)

---

## 7.2 Compositional Meaning

**Compositional Meaning**: The meaning of a phrase or sentence derived from its component parts.

- *[The] [tall] [man]* = combination of "the," "tall," and "man"
- The whole equals the sum of parts (mostly)

---

## 7.3 Ambiguity

**Lexical Ambiguity**: A word has multiple meanings
- Example: *bank* (financial vs. riverbank)

**Structural Ambiguity**: Sentence structure allows multiple interpretations
- Example: *Police help dog bite victim.* (Who bit whom?)

**Scope Ambiguity**: Quantifiers or operators can apply to different parts
- Example: *Every student read two books.* (2 books total or 2 per student?)

# LEVEL 8 ‚Äî PRAGMATICS & DISCOURSE

## 8.1 Information Structure

### Topic and Focus

**Topic**: What the sentence is about (often the subject)
- *The cats are sleeping.* (topic = "the cats")

**Focus**: The new or highlighted information
- *The cats are **sleeping**.* (focus = "sleeping")

---

## 8.2 Cohesion: Linking Ideas

### Reference (Anaphora)
- Use of pronouns to refer back to previous nouns
- Example: *John arrived. **He** was tired.* (He = John)

### Substitution
- Replacing a noun or phrase with a substitute
- Example: *Do you have a pen? Yes, I have one.* (one = pen)

### Ellipsis
- Omitting redundant information
- Example: *She likes coffee and [she likes] tea.* (implied repetition)

### Conjunction
- Using logical connectors to relate ideas
- Example: *It was raining, **so** we stayed inside.*

---

## 8.3 Speech Acts

**Speech Acts**: Actions performed through language.

### Austin's Five Categories

**Assertive**: Making statements that are true or false
- Example: *The sky is blue.*

**Directive**: Attempting to get the hearer to do something
- Example: *Close the door.* / *Can you help me?*

**Commissive**: Committing the speaker to a future action
- Example: *I promise to arrive on time.*

**Expressive**: Expressing a psychological state
- Example: *Thank you!* / *I'm sorry.*

**Declarative**: Changing reality through utterance
- Example: *I pronounce you married.*

---

## 8.4 Implicature

**Implicature**: Implied meaning beyond the literal words.

### Conversational Implicature
- What is implied by following (or breaking) Grice's maxims
- Example: *Can you pass the salt?* (literal = question about ability; implicature = request)

### Maxims of Cooperative Communication
- **Quantity**: Say as much as needed, but not more
- **Quality**: Say what you believe to be true
- **Relevance**: Say something relevant
- **Manner**: Be clear and orderly

---

## 8.5 Discourse Structure

### Coherence vs. Cohesion
- **Coherence**: Ideas make logical sense together
- **Cohesion**: Grammatical/lexical ties connect sentences

### Discourse Markers
- Words/phrases organizing discourse: *first, then, therefore, however, in conclusion*

### Turn-taking
- How speakers alternate in conversation
