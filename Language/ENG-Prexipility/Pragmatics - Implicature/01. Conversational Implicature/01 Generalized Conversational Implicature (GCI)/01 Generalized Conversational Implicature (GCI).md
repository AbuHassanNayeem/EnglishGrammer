Generalized Conversational Implicature (GCI)
├── **Definition**
│   ├── A type of conversational implicature that arises **systematically in most contexts** without relying on special contextual clues.
│   ├── Proposed by H.P. Grice as part of his theory of conversational implicature.
│   └── Contrasts with **Particularized Conversational Implicature (PCI)**, which depends heavily on context.
│
├── **Core Function**
│   ├── To convey meaning indirectly in a predictable, context-independent way.
│   ├── To allow efficient communication by exploiting **shared linguistic knowledge**.
│   ├── To create implicatures automatically in ordinary conversation.
│   └── Forms the basis for many **default pragmatic inferences** in exams.
│
├── **Main Types / Categories**
│   ├── **Scalar Implicature**
│   │   ├── **Definition**: Implicature arising from a **scale of informativeness** or strength in expressions (e.g., some → not all).
│   │   ├── **Function**: Indicates that a speaker chose a **less informative term**, implying stronger alternatives do not hold.
│   │   ├── **Examples**
│   │   │   ├── *Some students passed the exam.* → implies *Not all students passed.*  
│   │   │   └── *He is good at sports or music.* → may imply *not both*.  
│   │   ├── **Variations / Forms**
│   │   │   ├── Quantifiers: some, many, few  
│   │   │   ├── Modals: might, could  
│   │   │   └── Disjunctions: or  
│   │   ├── **Transformations**
│   │   │   └── Can be paraphrased explicitly: *He passed some, but not all, exams.*  
│   │   └── **Exam Tip**
│   │       └── “Check for weaker term → stronger alternative; common in SSC/BCS MCQs.”
│   │
│   ├── **Horn-Scale Implicature**
│   │   ├── **Definition**: Specialized scalar implicature using **Horn scales** (e.g., all > most > many > some > few).  
│   │   ├── **Function**: Predicts default inference based on **linguistic scales**.  
│   │   ├── **Examples**
│   │   │   ├── *Some students came.* → implicates *Not all came.*  
│   │   │   └── *He may come.* → implicates *He might not come.*  
│   │   └── **Exam Tip**
│   │       └── “Always visualize the scale from strongest → weakest; test often uses ‘some/all’ contrast.”
│   │
│   ├── **Quantitative / Proportional Implicature**
│   │   ├── **Definition**: Implicature based on **proportions or quantities** stated versus implied.  
│   │   ├── **Function**: Conveys partial information, allows inference about unmentioned quantities.  
│   │   ├── **Examples**
│   │   │   ├── *Some of the cookies were eaten.* → implies *Not all*.  
│   │   │   └── *Many students attended the lecture.* → may imply *Not everyone*.  
│   │   └── **Exam Tip**
│   │       └── “Look for numeric / quantity expressions; always contrast with total sets.”
│   │
│   ├── **Softening / Politeness Implicature**
│   │   ├── **Definition**: GCI that **reduces potential face-threat** in communication.  
│   │   ├── **Function**: Mitigates bluntness, conveys indirectness in requests or criticism.  
│   │   ├── **Examples**
│   │   │   ├── *Could you possibly help me?* → indirect request.  
│   │   │   └── *It’s a bit cold in here.* → implies *Please close the window*.  
│   │   └── **Exam Tip**
│   │       └── “Often appears in IELTS/TOEFL questions testing politeness and implicature recognition.”
│   │
│   ├── **Relevance / Manner-Based GCI**
│   │   ├── **Definition**: Implicature that is **context-independent** but guided by general conversational **maxims** (Relation, Manner).  
│   │   ├── **Function**: Ensures communication is informative, orderly, and non-ambiguous.  
│   │   ├── **Examples**
│   │   │   ├── *He went to Paris and Rome.* → implies sequence or combination.  
│   │   │   └── *John is poor but happy.* → implicates contrast.  
│   │   └── **Exam Tip**
│   │       └── “Contrastive connectors often signal GCI; check Relation & Manner maxim.”
│   │
│   └── **Conventionalized / Lexicalized GCI**
│       ├── **Definition**: Default implicature triggered by **lexical items**, but still context-independent.  
│       ├── **Function**: Enhances predictability in communication.  
│       ├── **Examples**
│       │   ├── *But* → contrast  
│       │   └── *Even* → emphasis / unexpectedness  
│       └── **Exam Tip**
│           └── “Lexical cues often tested in MCQs; recognize default implicature meanings.”
│
├── **Advanced / Edge Cases**
│   ├── Interaction with Particularized Conversational Implicature (PCI)  
│   │   └── GCI is default; PCI requires special context.  
│   ├── Cancellation
│   │   └── Some GCIs are **non-cancellable** (default implicature) → trick MCQs.  
│   ├── Embedded / Nested GCI  
│   │   └── Occurs in subordinate clauses or multi-clause sentences.  
│   ├── Cultural / Pragmatic Differences  
│   │   └── Some GCIs interpreted differently in formal vs informal registers.  
│   └── Interaction with Presupposition and Entailment  
│       └── GCI is **not presupposed**, can be overridden.
│
├── **Common Mistakes**
│   ├── Confusing GCI with PCI → context check is key.  
│   ├── Mistaking cancellable implicature for entailment.  
│   ├── Ignoring lexicalized/conventional cues.  
│   ├── Failing to apply scale reasoning in Horn-scale questions.  
│   └── Over-interpreting politeness as context-dependent implicature.
│
├── **Comparison with Similar Structures**
│   ├── **Particularized Conversational Implicature (PCI)**
│   │   ├── Context-dependent  
│   │   └── Usually cancellable  
│   ├── **Presupposition**
│   │   ├── Background assumption, survives negation  
│   │   └── GCI does not survive negation if cancelled  
│   └── **Entailment**
│       ├── Truth-preserving, non-cancellable  
│       └── GCI is inference-based, cancellable/non-cancellable depending on type
│
└── **Quick Revision Rules**
    ├── “GCI = default, predictable, context-independent.”  
    ├── “Check for scalar terms: some, all, many → Horn-scale reasoning.”  
    ├── “Contrastive connectors (*but, even*) often signal GCI.”  
    ├── “Softening/politeness implicature often tested in IELTS/TOEFL.”  
    ├── “Cancellable? Non-cancellable? Check type before answering MCQs.”  
    └── “Always distinguish GCI vs PCI vs presupposition vs entailment.”
