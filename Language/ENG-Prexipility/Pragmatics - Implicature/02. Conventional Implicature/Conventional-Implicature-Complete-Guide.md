## üéØ Executive Summary & Definition

### What is Conventional Implicature?

**Conventional implicature** refers to meaning that is linguistically encoded (built into specific words/constructions) but **does not affect truth conditions**. It is:

- **Proposed by Grice** as distinct from conversational implicature
- Meaning **fixed by lexical items**, independent of context
- Adds **extra meaning without affecting the truth value** of the sentence
- Independent of cooperative principles; arises from **lexical/conventional properties**
- Contrasts with conversational implicature (context-dependent and cancellable)

### The Three Defining Properties

#### ‚úÖ **1. Non-Cancellable**
- Cannot be negated without contradiction
- **Example**: *John is poor but happy.* ‚Üí "but" implies contrast; cannot cancel
- ‚ùå *John is poor but happy, but I don't mean to imply contrast.* (contradictory)
- **Exam Tip**: MCQs often test non-cancellability

#### ‚úÖ **2. Context-Independent**
- Meaning holds regardless of conversational context
- **Example**: *Even John passed the exam.* ‚Üí "even" conveys unexpectedness intrinsically
- Meaning is **lexically fixed**, not derived from context

#### ‚úÖ **3. Lexically Triggered**
- Specific words/particles trigger the implicature
- Common triggers: *but, even, yet, moreover, still, too, also, therefore, consequently, thus, hence, only, indeed, luckily, obviously*
- **Example**: *She is talented, yet humble.* ‚Üí "yet" triggers contrast
- **Exam Tip**: Memorize typical lexical triggers; often asked in SSC/BCS/IELTS

## üîë Core Properties

### Function / Role in Sentence

Conventional implicature:
- Provides **attitudinal, contrastive, evaluative, or inferential meaning**
- Signals speaker's perspective or logical connections
- Adds **additional meaning** without altering truth value of main clause
- Conveys **attitude, emphasis, contrast, or evaluation**
- Works **in parallel with entailment**; does not affect truth
- Maintains truth value of main clause
- Supports nuance, clarity, logical inference in formal and informal registers

**Example**: *John is poor but happy.*
- Truth of "John is happy" remains unchanged
- "but" adds conventional contrast

### Truth-Preserving Nature

Conventional implicatures **don't affect truth conditions**:
- *John is poor but happy.* ‚Üí Truth of "John is happy" unchanged by "but"
- *Only John passed.* ‚Üí Truth of "John passed" unchanged by "only"
- *Luckily, the train arrived on time.* ‚Üí Truth of arrival unchanged by "luckily"

## üì¶ The 6 Main Types (Simplified)

These are the **beginner-friendly categories** that provide a clear starting point:

### **Type 01: Contrastive Conjunctions**
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Definition**: Words that indicate contrast between clauses  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Function**: Highlight unexpected or opposing relationships; adds contrastive meaning  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Main Triggers**: but, yet, although, whereas, however, while  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Key Property**: Cannot replace with "and" without losing implicature  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Example**: *John is poor but happy.* ‚Üí "but" signals contrast  
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ **Cannot Be**: Replaced with "and" without losing contrastive meaning

**When to Use**:
- To highlight unexpected combinations or oppositions
- Academic writing: Use "however," "whereas," "although" for formality
- Casual speech: "but" is universal and neutral

---

### **Type 02: Focus-Sensitive Particles**
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Definition**: Words that emphasize unexpectedness, restriction, or emphasis  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Function**: Signal importance or highlight particular elements  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Main Triggers**: even, only, just, merely, also, too  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Key Property**: Position-sensitive (placement determines focus)  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Examples**:  
‚îÇ   ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ *Even John passed the exam.* ‚Üí emphasizes John's unexpected success  
‚îÇ   ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ *Only Mary attended the meeting.* ‚Üí restricts scope  
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ **Cannot Be**: Removed or paraphrased without losing meaning

**When to Use**:
- **even**: To signal surprise or low expectation
- **only**: To restrict to minimal/exclusive set
- **also/too**: To add to existing set (non-exclusive)

**Position Examples**:
- "Only JOHN passed" (others didn't)
- "John only PASSED" (didn't excel)
- "John passed ONLY" (and nothing else)

### **Type 03: Conclusive / Inferential Particles**
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Definition**: Words that indicate logical consequence or inference  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Function**: Signal reasoning or conclusion from prior statement  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Main Triggers**: therefore, consequently, thus, hence, so, accordingly (formal)  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Key Property**: Requires punctuation (semicolon/period before; comma after for formal)  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Examples**:  
‚îÇ   ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ *It rained; therefore, the match was canceled.*  
‚îÇ   ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ *She didn't study; consequently, she failed.*  
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ **Cannot Be**: Cancelled or ignored; meaning is lexical

**When to Use**:
- **Formal writing**: Use "therefore," "thus," "hence," "consequently"
- **Casual speech**: Use "so"
- To link premises to conclusions in arguments

**Formality Hierarchy**: hence/thus (formal) > therefore > so (informal)

### **Type 04: Additive Particles**
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Definition**: Words that add information or include additional elements  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Function**: Provide additional meaning without affecting clause truth  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Main Triggers**: and, also, too, moreover, furthermore, in addition, plus  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Key Property**: Presupposes non-uniqueness (others exist)  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Examples**:  
‚îÇ   ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ *John is kind and smart.* ‚Üí "and" adds information  
‚îÇ   ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ *She speaks French and also German.* ‚Üí "also" adds to the list  
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ **Cannot Be**: Removed without losing additive implicature

**When to Use**:
- **also/too**: To add to existing set (also = mid-sentence; too = sentence-final)
- **moreover/furthermore**: To escalate or strengthen argument (formal)
- **and**: Universal neutral addition

**Example with Presupposition**: 
- *John also passed.* ‚Üí Presupposes others passed

### **Type 05: Concessive Particles**
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Definition**: Words that indicate concession or despite expectations  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Function**: Conveys concession, despite expectations; acknowledges obstacle but asserts outcome persists  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Main Triggers**: still, nevertheless, nonetheless, yet, even so, all the same  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Key Property**: Formal particles need period/semicolon before  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Examples**:  
‚îÇ   ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ *It was raining; still, they went out.*  
‚îÇ   ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ *She tried hard; nevertheless, she failed.*  
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ **Cannot Be**: Cancelled or ignored; meaning is lexical

**When to Use**:
- **Formal writing**: Use "nevertheless," "nonetheless"
- **Neutral contexts**: Use "still," "yet," "even so"
- **Casual speech**: Use "all the same"
- To acknowledge counterpoints while maintaining position

---

### **Type 06: Evaluative / Attitudinal Particles**
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Definition**: Words that express speaker's attitude or evaluation toward the proposition  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Function**: Conveys speaker attitude, emotion, or judgment; truth-independent  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Main Triggers**: luckily, unfortunately, frankly, obviously, surprisingly, naturally, wisely, foolishly  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Key Property**: Truth-independent (doesn't change propositional truth)  
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Examples**:  
‚îÇ   ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ *Luckily, the train arrived on time.* ‚Üí "luckily" conveys positive evaluation  
‚îÇ   ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ *Unfortunately, he missed the bus.* ‚Üí "unfortunately" conveys negative evaluation  
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ **Cannot Be**: Cancelled without contradiction

**When to Use**:
- **Positive evaluation**: luckily, fortunately, wisely, naturally
- **Negative evaluation**: unfortunately, unluckily, foolishly, regrettably, sadly
- **Candor**: frankly, honestly (for uncomfortable truths)
- **Certainty**: obviously, clearly (for self-evident claims)
- **Surprise**: surprisingly, unexpectedly (for violated expectations)

## üìÇ Extended Taxonomy (15 Advanced Categories)

The 6 core types expand into this **comprehensive classification system** for advanced study:

### **Category 01: Contrastive & Adversative Markers**

#### **1.1 Basic Contrastives**

- **Markers**: but, yet, however, whereas, while, nevertheless, nonetheless

- **Function**: Signal semantic opposition + denial of expectation between clauses
  - Highlight unexpected or opposing relationships
  - Add contrastive meaning without affecting truth conditions
  - Signal that expectations are violated

- **Expectation Structure**: P but Q ‚Üí normally (P ‚Üí ¬¨Q)
  - The use of contrastive markers implies that P and Q are typically incompatible or unexpected together

- **Example Analysis**:
  - **Sentence**: "John is poor but happy"
  - **At-issue content**: John is poor AND John is happy (both are true)
  - **Conventional Implicature**: Poverty is typically incompatible with happiness; this combination is unexpected

- **Position**: Can be clause-initial, medial, or final depending on the marker
  - Clause-medial: "John is poor **but** happy"
  - Clause-initial: "**However**, he succeeded"
  - Sentence-final: "He failed, **however**"

- **Formality Hierarchy**: 
  - Most formal ‚Üí Least formal: notwithstanding > nevertheless > nonetheless > however > whereas > while > yet > but
  - Academic writing: Use "however," "whereas," "nevertheless," "nonetheless"
  - Neutral contexts: Use "but," "yet," "while"
  - Casual speech: "but" is universal and neutral

- **Punctuation Rules**:
  - **but, yet**: No comma before when joining two independent clauses in same sentence
  - **however, nevertheless, nonetheless**: Semicolon or period before; comma after
    - Example: "It rained; however, they went out."
  - **whereas, while**: Comma before when contrasting clauses

**When to Use**:
- To highlight unexpected combinations or oppositions
- To signal denial of normal expectations
- To create contrast between two propositions
- Academic writing: Prefer formal variants for sophistication
- Casual speech: "but" and "yet" are universally appropriate

**Examples by Marker**:

**BUT** (Most common, neutral):
- "She's young but wise" (youth ‚â† wisdom expected)
- "He tried but failed" (effort ‚Üí success expected)
- "It's cheap but good" (cheapness ‚â† quality expected)
- "John is poor but happy" (poverty ‚â† happiness expected)

**YET** (Emphasizes ongoing contrast):
- "She's intelligent yet humble" (intelligence ‚â† humility expected)
- "He studied hard yet failed" (hard study ‚Üí success expected)
- "The task is simple yet challenging" (simplicity ‚â† challenge expected)

**HOWEVER** (Formal, sentence connector):
- "John worked hard. However, he didn't succeed." (effort ‚Üí success expected)
- "She's wealthy. However, she's unhappy." (wealth ‚Üí happiness expected)
- "The plan seemed perfect; however, it failed." (perfection ‚Üí success expected)

**WHEREAS** (Formal, strong contrast):
- "John is poor whereas Mary is rich" (direct comparison of opposing states)
- "I prefer tea whereas she prefers coffee" (contrasting preferences)
- "He's extroverted whereas his brother is introverted" (opposite personalities)

**WHILE** (Simultaneous contrast):
- "John is poor while Mary is rich" (simultaneous contrasting states)
- "I enjoy reading while he enjoys sports" (different activities/preferences)
- "She's optimistic while he's pessimistic" (contrasting attitudes)

**NEVERTHELESS** (Formal, strong concession):
- "The task was difficult; nevertheless, she completed it." (difficulty ‚Üí non-completion expected)
- "He was tired; nevertheless, he continued working." (tiredness ‚Üí stopping expected)

**NONETHELESS** (Formal, emphatic concession):
- "The evidence was weak; nonetheless, they convicted him." (weak evidence ‚Üí acquittal expected)
- "She's young; nonetheless, she's very wise." (youth ‚â† wisdom expected)

**Common Exam Traps**:
- ‚ùå Substituting "but" with "and": Changes meaning entirely
  - "John is poor and happy" (simple conjunction, no contrast)
  - "John is poor but happy" (contrastive, unexpected combination)
- ‚ùå Incorrect punctuation with "however": *"It rained, however they went out."* ‚Üí ‚úÖ "It rained; however, they went out."
- ‚ùå Attempting to cancel the contrast: *"John is poor but happy, but I don't mean there's contrast."* (contradictory)

#### **1.2 Concessive Markers**

- **Markers**: although, though, even though, despite, in spite of, notwithstanding

- **Function**: Acknowledge obstacle or contrary condition but assert main clause anyway
  - Signal that an expected consequence does not occur
  - Convey that the obstacle was overcome or ignored
  - More explicit about acknowledging the difficulty than simple contrastives

- **Expectation Structure**: Although P (obstacle), Q (unexpected outcome given P)
  - The concessive marker explicitly acknowledges P as a potential barrier to Q
  - Q occurs despite P being present

- **Example Analysis**:
  - **Sentence**: "Although tired, she continued working"
  - **At-issue content**: She continued working
  - **Conventional Implicature**: Tiredness would normally cause someone to stop working; this didn't happen

- **Position**: 
  - Clause-initial: "**Although** tired, she continued working"
  - Clause-medial: "She continued working **although** she was tired"
  - Clause-final: "She continued working, **though** she was tired"

- **Formality Hierarchy**:
  - Most formal ‚Üí Least formal: notwithstanding > despite > in spite of > even though > although > though
  - Academic writing: Use "although," "despite," "notwithstanding"
  - Neutral writing: Use "although," "even though"
  - Casual speech: Use "though"

- **Structural Differences**:
  - **although/though/even though**: Followed by clause (subject + verb)
    - "Although **she was tired**, she continued"
  - **despite/in spite of/notwithstanding**: Followed by noun phrase
    - "Despite **her tiredness**, she continued"
    - "In spite of **being tired**, she continued"

- **Punctuation Rules**:
  - Comma after introductory concessive clause
    - "Although he was tired**,** he continued."
  - Comma before concessive clause at end
    - "He continued**,** although he was tired."

**When to Use**:
- To acknowledge obstacles or counterarguments while maintaining your position
- To emphasize persistence despite difficulty
- To show that expected consequences didn't occur
- Academic writing: Demonstrates nuanced argumentation
- Casual speech: "though" is most common

**Examples by Marker**:

**ALTHOUGH** (Standard, neutral formality):
- "Although tired, she continued working" (tiredness ‚Üí stopping expected)
- "Although it rained, they went hiking" (rain ‚Üí cancellation expected)
- "Although expensive, it's worth buying" (high cost ‚Üí not buying expected)
- "Although young, he's very mature" (youth ‚â† maturity expected)

**THOUGH** (Casual variant of although):
- "Though tired, she continued working" (tiredness ‚Üí stopping expected)
- "Though he studied little, he passed" (little study ‚Üí failure expected)
- "Though small, the apartment is cozy" (small size ‚Üí uncomfortable expected)

**EVEN THOUGH** (Emphatic, stresses unexpectedness):
- "Even though exhausted, she finished the race" (exhaustion ‚Üí quitting expected)
- "Even though warned, he proceeded anyway" (warning ‚Üí caution expected)
- "Even though rich, he lives frugally" (wealth ‚Üí lavish lifestyle expected)

**DESPITE** (Formal, requires noun phrase):
- "Despite her tiredness, she continued working" (tiredness ‚Üí stopping expected)
- "Despite the rain, they went hiking" (rain ‚Üí cancellation expected)
- "Despite warnings, he proceeded" (warnings ‚Üí caution expected)
- "Despite being young, he's mature" (youth ‚â† maturity expected)

**IN SPITE OF** (Formal, synonym of despite):
- "In spite of her tiredness, she continued working" (tiredness ‚Üí stopping expected)
- "In spite of the difficulties, they succeeded" (difficulties ‚Üí failure expected)
- "In spite of opposition, the plan proceeded" (opposition ‚Üí abandonment expected)

**NOTWITHSTANDING** (Very formal, legal/academic):
- "Notwithstanding her tiredness, she continued working" (tiredness ‚Üí stopping expected)
- "Notwithstanding the objections, the motion passed" (objections ‚Üí rejection expected)
- "Notwithstanding the risks, they invested" (risks ‚Üí avoidance expected)
- Can also be used postpositively: "Her tiredness notwithstanding, she continued"

**Common Exam Traps**:
- ‚ùå Using clause after "despite": *"Despite she was tired"* ‚Üí ‚úÖ "Despite being tired" OR "Although she was tired"
- ‚ùå Using noun phrase after "although": *"Although tiredness"* ‚Üí ‚úÖ "Although tired" OR "Despite tiredness"
- ‚ùå Missing comma with introductory concessive: *"Although tired she continued"* ‚Üí ‚úÖ "Although tired**,** she continued"

#### **1.3 Corrective & Replacive Markers**

- **Markers**: rather, instead, on the contrary, by contrast, not X but Y, or rather

- **Function**: Correct or replace a previous proposition or characterization
  - Signal metalinguistic negation (rejecting the form of expression)
  - Explicitly reject one option in favor of another
  - More corrective than simple opposition
  - Often involves precision or accuracy concerns

- **Expectation Structure**: Not P (rejected), but rather Q (correct alternative)
  - Explicitly denies P and replaces it with Q
  - Implies that P was incorrect, imprecise, or inappropriate
  - Stronger than simple contrast; involves correction

- **Example Analysis**:
  - **Sentence**: "He's not sad, but rather disappointed"
  - **At-issue content**: He is disappointed (not sad)
  - **Conventional Implicature**: The distinction between "sad" and "disappointed" is meaningful and important; "sad" was wrong or imprecise

- **Position**:
  - Clause-medial (most common): "not X **but rather** Y"
  - Clause-initial: "**On the contrary**, he succeeded"
  - Reformulation: "He's tall‚Äî**or rather**, very tall"

- **Formality Hierarchy**:
  - Most formal ‚Üí Least formal: on the contrary > by contrast > rather > instead > or rather
  - Academic writing: "on the contrary," "by contrast," "rather"
  - Neutral contexts: "rather," "instead"
  - Self-correction: "or rather"

- **Structural Patterns**:
  - **not X but Y**: "He's not tall but short"
  - **not X but rather Y**: "He's not sad but rather disappointed"
  - **X. On the contrary, Y**: "He didn't fail. On the contrary, he excelled."
  - **X. Instead, Y**: "He didn't walk. Instead, he drove."
  - **X‚Äîor rather, Y**: "He's tall‚Äîor rather, very tall."

**When to Use**:
- To correct a misconception or imprecision
- To replace one characterization with a more accurate one
- To reject a proposition and supply the correct alternative
- In arguments: To refute and replace an opponent's claim
- Self-correction: "or rather" for online refinement

**Examples by Marker**:

**RATHER** (Standard correction):
- "He's not sad, but rather disappointed" (replacing emotion term with more precise one)
- "She didn't run, but rather jogged" (correcting verb choice)
- "It's not expensive, but rather overpriced" (replacing neutral with evaluative term)
- "He's not a teacher, but rather a professor" (correcting title/role)

**INSTEAD** (Alternative action/state):
- "He didn't walk. Instead, he drove." (replacing one action with another)
- "She didn't accept. Instead, she declined." (opposite action taken)
- "They didn't fight. Instead, they compromised." (alternative approach)
- "He didn't resign. Instead, he was fired." (correcting what happened)

**ON THE CONTRARY** (Formal, strong opposition):
- "He didn't fail. On the contrary, he excelled." (complete opposite outcome)
- "The plan isn't weak. On the contrary, it's robust." (strong opposite evaluation)
- "She's not lazy. On the contrary, she's very diligent." (opposite trait)
- "It's not simple. On the contrary, it's quite complex." (opposite characteristic)

**BY CONTRAST** (Formal, comparative correction):
- "The first attempt failed. By contrast, the second succeeded." (opposite outcomes in comparison)
- "The first candidate was weak. By contrast, the second was strong." (opposite characteristics)
- "Urban areas grew. By contrast, rural areas declined." (opposite trends)

**NOT X BUT Y** (Direct replacement):
- "He's not smart but clever" (replacing one positive term with another)
- "She's not rich but wealthy" (similar but more precise)
- "It's not difficult but challenging" (reframing the characterization)
- "He's not a liar but a storyteller" (euphemistic replacement)

**OR RATHER** (Self-correction, refinement):
- "He's tall‚Äîor rather, very tall" (refining degree)
- "She's smart‚Äîor rather, brilliant" (upgrading assessment)
- "It's expensive‚Äîor rather, outrageously expensive" (intensifying)
- "He's my friend‚Äîor rather, my best friend" (specifying relationship)

**Metalinguistic Negation**:
Corrective markers often involve rejecting not just the content but the form:
- "He's not /Ààek…ônom…™k/ but /ÀåiÀêk…ôÀàn…ím…™k/" (pronunciation correction)
- "It's not 'irregardless' but 'regardless'" (word choice correction)
- "She didn't 'borrow' it from me, she **stole** it" (euphemism rejected)

**Common Exam Traps**:
- ‚ùå Confusing "rather" (correction) with "somewhat" (degree): 
  - "It's rather expensive" (somewhat)
  - "It's not cheap but rather expensive" (correction)
- ‚ùå Using "on the contrary" when not truly opposite: 
  - ‚ùå "He's tall. On the contrary, his brother is short." (use "by contrast" instead)
  - ‚úÖ "He's not short. On the contrary, he's very tall."
- ‚ùå Missing "but" with "rather": *"He's not sad, rather disappointed"* ‚Üí ‚úÖ "not sad, **but** rather disappointed"

### **Category 02: Scalar & Focus-Sensitive Particles**

#### **2.1 Scalar Endpoint Particles**

- **Markers**: even, at least, at most, as much as, no less than, as many as

- **Function**: Mark extreme points on contextual scales; signal scalar implicatures
  - Indicate that the focused element is at the extreme end of a scale (likelihood, expectedness, remarkability)
  - Trigger both **additive** and **scalar** presuppositions
  - Emphasize unexpectedness or surprise
  - Place items relative to alternatives on an implied scale

- **Scalar Structure**: 
  - Items are ranked on a scale: least likely ‚Üí most likely
  - "Even" marks the least likely/expected point
  - "At least/most" marks boundaries on quantity scales

- **Example Analysis**:
  - **Sentence**: "Even John passed the exam"
  - **At-issue content**: John passed the exam
  - **Additive presupposition**: Someone else (other than John) also passed
  - **Scalar presupposition**: John was the least likely person to pass (among relevant alternatives)
  - **Conventional Implicature**: Passing was surprising/unexpected for John specifically

- **The Two Components**:
  1. **Additive Component**: Others in the set also have the property
     - "Even **John** passed" ‚Üí Others also passed
  2. **Scalar Component**: The focused element is ranked lowest on the likelihood scale
     - "Even **John** passed" ‚Üí John was least likely to pass

- **Position & Scope**: Position of "even" affects meaning
  - "John even didn't **TRY**" (emphasis on the minimal action)
  - "John **didn't even** try" (negation of minimal expectation)
  - "**Even John** didn't try" (John was least expected to fail to try)

- **Formality Hierarchy**:
  - Formal ‚Üí Informal: no less than > as much as > even > at least/at most
  - Academic writing: "no less than," "as much as," "at least/at most"
  - Neutral contexts: "even," "at least," "at most"
  - Casual speech: "even" is universal

**When to Use**:
- To emphasize unexpectedness or surprise
- To mark the extreme/least likely element in a set
- To create emphasis on minimal or maximal boundaries
- To signal violated expectations

**Examples by Marker**:

**EVEN** (Most common, marks surprise/low expectation):
- "Even **I** know that" (I'm the least knowledgeable, so others definitely know)
  - Scalar: I'm at the bottom of knowledge scale
  - Additive: Others also know this
- "Even **the expert** was confused" (if expert is confused, others certainly are)
  - Scalar: Expert is at top of knowledge scale; confusion unexpected
  - Additive: Others were also confused
- "She didn't invite even **her best friend**" (if not best friend, then no one)
  - Scalar: Best friend is most likely to be invited
  - Negation + even = complete exclusion
- "Even **in summer**, it's cold here" (summer is warmest; still cold)
  - Scalar: Summer is extreme on warmth scale

**AT LEAST** (Minimal boundary marker):
- "At least **ten people** attended" (10 is minimum; possibly more)
  - Sets lower bound on quantity scale
  - Implicates: Actual number ‚â• 10
- "She's at least **honest**" (minimal positive quality)
  - Sets minimal expectation for positive traits
  - Implicates: May lack other qualities
- "It costs at least **$100**" (minimum price)
  - Lower boundary on cost scale

**AT MOST** (Maximal boundary marker):
- "At most **five people** can fit" (5 is maximum; possibly fewer)
  - Sets upper bound on quantity scale
  - Implicates: Actual number ‚â§ 5
- "It will take at most **two hours**" (maximum duration)
  - Upper boundary on time scale
- "He's at most **a novice**" (low on expertise scale)
  - Sets maximum level of competence

**AS MUCH AS / AS MANY AS** (Emphasizing large quantity):
- "As many as **50 people** attended" (50 is surprisingly high)
  - Emphasizes unexpectedly large number
  - Implicates: Speaker finds quantity remarkable
- "It cost as much as **$1000**" (surprisingly expensive)
  - Emphasizes high point on cost scale

**NO LESS THAN** (Formal, emphasizing minimum):
- "No less than **the president** attended" (highest-ranking person)
  - Emphasizes importance/rank
  - Very formal register
- "No less than **100 people** came" (surprisingly large, formal)

**Scope Ambiguities & Word Order**:
The position of "even" critically affects interpretation:

1. **Subject focus**: "**Even John** passed"
   - John was least likely to pass

2. **Verb focus**: "John **even passed**"  
   - Passing was minimal achievement (didn't excel)

3. **Object focus**: "John passed **even the hardest exam**"
   - Hardest exam is extreme on difficulty scale

4. **Negation interactions**:
   - "He didn't **even try**" (negative polarity, didn't do minimum)
   - "**Even he** didn't try" (he was least expected to fail)

**Common Exam Traps**:
- ‚ùå Confusing additive and scalar: "Even John passed" means:
  - ‚úÖ Others also passed (additive)
  - ‚úÖ John was least likely (scalar)
  - ‚ùå Only John passed (wrong!)
  
- ‚ùå Wrong position: *"I even know that"* vs *"Even I know that"*
  - First: Emphasizes knowing (minimal action)
  - Second: Emphasizes I (least likely knower) ‚úÖ for surprise

- ‚ùå Misunderstanding "at least" as exactness:
  - "At least 10 people came" ‚â† exactly 10
  - Means: 10 or more ‚úÖ

#### **2.2 Restrictive & Exclusive Particles**

- **Markers**: only, just, merely, simply, solely, exclusively, alone, but (in "nothing but")

- **Function**: Exclude all alternatives from consideration; mark exhaustive identification
  - Signal that focused element is the **sole member** satisfying the predicate
  - Create **exclusive** (not inclusive) meaning
  - **Presuppose** existence of focused element
  - **Assert** that no alternatives hold

- **Logical Structure**:
  - **Presupposition**: At least X has property P
  - **Assertion**: Nothing/no one except X has property P
  - **Implicature**: All alternatives to X are excluded

- **Example Analysis**:
  - **Sentence**: "Only John passed"
  - **At-issue content**: John passed
  - **Presupposition**: At least John passed (existence)
  - **Assertion**: No one else passed (exclusion)
  - **Implicature**: Exhaustive identification‚ÄîJohn and no others

- **Position = Meaning**: The position of "only" determines what is excluded
  - **Subject focus**: "**Only JOHN** passed" ‚Üí No one else passed
  - **Verb focus**: "John **only PASSED**" ‚Üí He didn't excel/ace it
  - **Object focus**: "John passed **only THE EXAM**" ‚Üí He didn't pass anything else
  - **Modifier focus**: "John **only passed YESTERDAY**" ‚Üí He didn't pass before/after

- **Formality Hierarchy**:
  - Most formal ‚Üí Least formal: exclusively > solely > merely > simply > only > just
  - Academic writing: "solely," "exclusively," "merely"
  - Neutral contexts: "only"
  - Casual speech: "just," "only"

- **Focus Structure**: Prosody (stress pattern) determines scope
  - "Only **JOHN** passed the exam" (stressed JOHN)
  - "Only John **PASSED** the exam" (stressed PASSED)
  - Written context may use capitals, italics, or word order

**When to Use**:
- To restrict to a single element or minimal set
- To exclude all alternatives
- To emphasize limitation or minimality
- To create precise, exclusive identification

**Examples by Marker**:

**ONLY** (Standard, neutral exclusion):
- "**Only John** passed" (exclusion of others)
  - Presupposes: John passed
  - Asserts: No one else passed
  
- "John **only passed**" (minimal achievement)
  - Presupposes: John passed
  - Asserts: He didn't excel/ace
  
- "John passed **only the exam**" (exclusion of other assessments)
  - Presupposes: John passed the exam
  - Asserts: He didn't pass assignments/projects/etc.

- "**Only yesterday** did he arrive" (temporal exclusion)
  - Presupposes: He arrived yesterday
  - Asserts: Not before or after

**JUST** (Casual, often minimizing):
- "**Just five people** came" (small number, only 5)
  - Exclusive: No more than 5
  - Often implies: Disappointingly few
  
- "I **just wanted** to help" (minimal intention)
  - Exclusive: No ulterior motive
  - Minimizes: Simple, uncomplicated desire

- "It's **just a scratch**" (minimizing severity)
  - Exclusive: Nothing more serious
  - Downplays: Not a big deal

**MERELY** (Formal, minimizing/diminishing):
- "He was **merely a clerk**" (low status, nothing higher)
  - Exclusive: Not manager/executive
  - Diminishes: Low-ranking position
  
- "It's **merely a suggestion**" (not binding)
  - Exclusive: Not a requirement/demand
  - Minimizes: Non-obligatory

- "This is **merely the beginning**" (minimal stage)
  - Exclusive: Not the end/middle
  - Implies: More to come

**SIMPLY** (Emphasis on straightforwardness):
- "It's **simply impossible**" (emphatic exclusion)
  - Exclusive: Not possible at all
  - Emphasizes: Absolute impossibility
  
- "I **simply forgot**" (uncomplicated reason)
  - Exclusive: No complex explanation
  - Straightforward: Just forgetfulness

**SOLELY** (Formal, absolute exclusion):
- "The decision rests **solely** with you" (complete exclusivity)
  - Exclusive: No one else has authority
  - Formal tone
  
- "He acts **solely** in self-interest" (single motivation)
  - Exclusive: No other motivations
  - Often negative connotation

**EXCLUSIVELY** (Very formal, complete restriction):
- "This offer is **exclusively** for members" (complete restriction)
  - Exclusive: Non-members cannot access
  - Very formal
  
- "She works **exclusively** with children" (specialized restriction)
  - Exclusive: Doesn't work with adults
  - Professional context

**ALONE** (Emphatic isolation):
- "**John alone** knows the truth" (emphatic sole knower)
  - Exclusive: No one else knows
  - Emphatic position (postpositive possible: "John alone" = "Only John")

- "He lives by bread **alone**" (used postpositively)
  - Exclusive: Nothing else needed
  - Literary/poetic

**Scope Interactions - Critical for Exams**:

| Sentence | What's Excluded | Meaning |
|:---------|:----------------|:--------|
| **Only JOHN** passed the exam | Other people | John passed; others didn't |
| John **only PASSED** the exam | Higher achievements (ace, excel) | John passed but didn't do better |
| John passed **only THE EXAM** | Other assessments | John passed exam but not projects/assignments |
| John passed the exam **only YESTERDAY** | Other times | John passed yesterday, not before/after |

**Common Exam Traps**:
- ‚ùå Verb agreement error: *"Only John **pass**"* ‚Üí ‚úÖ "Only John **passes**" (singular verb)

- ‚ùå Wrong position changes meaning:
  - "I only **love** you" (I don't hate/like you, just love)
  - "I love only **you**" (I don't love others) ‚úÖ More common intended meaning

- ‚ùå Confusing "only" (exclusive) with "also" (inclusive):
  - "Only John passed" = John alone (exclusive)
  - "John also passed" = John + others (inclusive)

- ‚ùå Double negative with "only": 
  - *"Only not John passed"* ‚Üí Grammatically awkward
  - ‚úÖ "Everyone but John passed" OR "Only John didn't pass"

#### **2.3 Additive & Inclusive Particles**

- **Markers**: also, too, as well, additionally, likewise, moreover, furthermore, in addition, plus, besides

- **Function**: Include element in existing set; signal non-uniqueness
  - **Presuppose** that at least one other entity has the property
  - **Assert** that the focused element also has the property
  - Signal **addition** rather than exclusion
  - Create **inclusive** (not exclusive) meaning

- **Logical Structure**:
  - **Presupposition**: Someone/something else has property P
  - **Assertion**: X also has property P
  - **Implicature**: X is not unique in having P; non-exhaustive

- **Example Analysis**:
  - **Sentence**: "Mary also passed"
  - **At-issue content**: Mary passed
  - **Presupposition**: At least one other person (besides Mary) passed
  - **Implicature**: Mary is one of multiple people who passed

- **Position Constraints** (Important for exams):
  - **also**: Mid-sentence, before verb or after auxiliary
    - "Mary **also** passed" ‚úÖ
    - "Mary passed also" ‚ùå (ungrammatical)
    
  - **too**: Clause-final or sentence-final
    - "Mary passed **too**" ‚úÖ
    - "Mary too passed" ‚ùå (ungrammatical except in literary style)
    
  - **as well**: Clause-final
    - "Mary passed **as well**" ‚úÖ
    - "As well Mary passed" ‚ùå

- **Formality Hierarchy**:
  - Most formal ‚Üí Least formal: moreover > furthermore > additionally > likewise > besides > in addition > also > as well > too > plus
  - Academic writing: "moreover," "furthermore," "additionally," "likewise"
  - Neutral writing: "also," "in addition," "besides"
  - Casual speech: "too," "as well," "plus"

- **Prosodic Focus**: The particle associates with the focused element
  - "**MARY** also passed" (focus on Mary; others passed too)
  - "Mary also **PASSED**" (focus on passing; she did other things too)

**When to Use**:
- To add information to an existing set
- To signal non-uniqueness (others exist)
- To build cumulative lists or arguments
- To show shared properties across entities

**Examples by Marker**:

**ALSO** (Standard, mid-sentence):
- "**John** also passed" (others passed; John is added to the set)
  - Presupposes: At least one other person passed
  - Asserts: John passed
  
- "John also **speaks French**" (he speaks other languages too)
  - Presupposes: John speaks another language
  - Asserts: John speaks French

- "**I** also agree" (others agree; I'm added)
  - Presupposes: At least one other person agrees
  - Asserts: I agree

**TOO** (Casual, sentence-final):
- "John passed **too**" (others passed; John included)
  - Presupposes: At least one other person passed
  - Asserts: John passed
  - Position: Must be sentence-final in modern English

- "I want to go **too**" (others want to go)
  - Presupposes: At least one other person wants to go
  - Asserts: I want to go

**AS WELL** (Neutral, sentence-final):
- "Mary passed **as well**" (others passed; Mary included)
  - Presupposes: At least one other person passed
  - Asserts: Mary passed
  - Slightly more formal than "too"

- "He's intelligent **as well**" (he has other positive qualities)
  - Presupposes: He has other qualities
  - Asserts: Intelligence is one of them

**ADDITIONALLY** (Formal, discourse connector):
- "John passed. **Additionally**, he won an award." (adding information)
  - Presupposes: Prior information provided
  - Asserts: New information added
  - Creates cohesive discourse

**LIKEWISE** (Formal, similarity marker):
- "John passed. **Likewise**, Mary passed." (parallel action)
  - Presupposes: John's passing established
  - Asserts: Mary's passing is similar/parallel
  - Emphasizes similarity or parallel structure

**MOREOVER** (Formal, escalating):
- "He's smart. **Moreover**, he's hardworking." (stronger addition)
  - Presupposes: Intelligence established
  - Asserts: Adds work ethic
  - Often escalates or strengthens argument

**FURTHERMORE** (Formal, continuing argument):
- "The plan is risky. **Furthermore**, it's expensive." (additional negative)
  - Presupposes: Risk established
  - Asserts: Cost is additional concern
  - Builds cumulative case

**IN ADDITION** (Formal, explicit addition):
- "He's talented. **In addition**, he's experienced." (explicit additive)
  - Presupposes: Talent established
  - Asserts: Experience is additional quality
  - Very explicit about adding information

**BESIDES** (Neutral, often dismissive):
- "I don't want to. **Besides**, I'm busy." (additional reason)
  - Presupposes: First reason given
  - Asserts: Second reason provided
  - Often implies: Even if first reason weren't enough

**PLUS** (Very casual, conversational):
- "It's expensive, **plus** it's ugly." (casual addition)
  - Presupposes: Cost established
  - Asserts: Appearance is additional negative
  - Very informal; avoid in academic writing

**Position Rules** (Critical for Exams):

| Particle | Position | Example |
|:---------|:---------|:--------|
| **also** | Before main verb; after auxiliary | "He **also** went" / "He has **also** gone" |
| **too** | Clause-final | "He went **too**" |
| **as well** | Clause-final | "He went **as well**" |
| **moreover** | Sentence-initial | "**Moreover**, he went" |
| **furthermore** | Sentence-initial | "**Furthermore**, he went" |
| **in addition** | Sentence-initial | "**In addition**, he went" |
| **besides** | Sentence-initial or clause-final | "**Besides**, he went" / "He went, **besides**" |

**Common Exam Traps**:
- ‚ùå Redundant use: *"John also passed too"* ‚Üí ‚úÖ Use ONE: "John also passed" OR "John passed too"

- ‚ùå Wrong position: *"Mary passed also"* ‚Üí ‚úÖ "Mary **also** passed" (also before verb)

- ‚ùå Wrong position: *"Too Mary passed"* ‚Üí ‚úÖ "Mary passed **too**" (too at end)

- ‚ùå Formal/informal mismatch:
  - ‚ùå "The results were significant, **plus** further research is warranted." (academic context)
  - ‚úÖ "The results were significant. **Moreover**, further research is warranted."

- ‚ùå Confusing "also" (additive) with "only" (exclusive):
  - "John also passed" = John + others (inclusive)
  - "Only John passed" = John alone (exclusive)

#### **2.4 Scalar Comparison Constructions**

- **Markers**: not only...but (also), not just...but, let alone, much less, still less, not to mention, even less

- **Function**: Establish scalar ordering between propositions; mark comparative implicature
  - Rank two propositions on a scale of likelihood, difficulty, or remarkability
  - **Assert** both propositions (in positive constructions)
  - Create **scalar inference** about relative positioning
  - Signal **a fortiori reasoning** (if extreme case holds, less extreme certainly holds)

- **Scalar Logic**:
  - Two propositions P and Q are ordered: Q is more extreme/unexpected than P
  - **Positive**: "Not only P, but (also) Q" ‚Üí Both P and Q are true; Q is more surprising
  - **Negative**: "He can't even do P, let alone Q" ‚Üí Neither P nor Q; Q is more difficult

- **Example Analysis - Positive Construction**:
  - **Sentence**: "He not only won but broke the record"
  - **At-issue content**: He won AND he broke the record (both true)
  - **Scalar implicature**: Breaking the record is more remarkable/unexpected than merely winning
  - **Ordering**: Winning < Breaking record (on remarkability scale)

- **Example Analysis - Negative Construction**:
  - **Sentence**: "He can't walk, let alone run"
  - **At-issue content**: He can't walk (asserted directly)
  - **Implicature**: He certainly can't run (inferred)
  - **Presupposition**: Running is harder than walking
  - **Ordering**: Walking < Running (on difficulty scale)

**When to Use**:
- To emphasize escalation from lesser to greater achievement
- To show progression on a scale
- To create emphasis through scalar comparison
- To make a fortiori arguments (if Q, then certainly P)

**Examples by Construction**:

**NOT ONLY...BUT (ALSO)** (Positive escalation):

**Structure**: "not only P, but (also) Q" where Q > P on relevant scale

- "He **not only passed** but **aced the exam**"
  - P: passed (less impressive)
  - Q: aced (more impressive)
  - Both are true; Q is more remarkable

- "She **not only apologized** but **also paid compensation**"
  - P: apologized (verbal)
  - Q: paid compensation (concrete action)
  - Escalation from words to deeds

- "It's **not only expensive** but **also illegal**"
  - P: expensive (practical concern)
  - Q: illegal (moral/legal concern)
  - Escalation in severity

**Position variants**:
- "Not only did he win, but he broke the record" (auxiliary inversion)
- "He not only won but also set a new standard" (parallel structure)

**NOT JUST...BUT** (Similar to not only, slightly less formal):

- "It's **not just difficult** but **impossible**"
  - P: difficult (challenging but doable)
  - Q: impossible (undoable)
  - Escalation on feasibility scale

- "He's **not just smart** but **brilliant**"
  - P: smart (intelligent)
  - Q: brilliant (exceptionally intelligent)
  - Escalation on intelligence scale

**LET ALONE** (Negative polarity, a fortiori reasoning):

**Structure**: "can't/won't do P, let alone Q" where Q > P on difficulty scale

**Critical**: Requires **negative or non-affirmative** context

- "He **can't walk, let alone run**"
  - P: walk (easier)
  - Q: run (harder)
  - Presupposes: running > walking on difficulty scale
  - Asserts: can't walk
  - Implicates: certainly can't run

- "She **won't speak to me, let alone help me**"
  - P: speak (minimal interaction)
  - Q: help (greater interaction)
  - Presupposes: helping > speaking on commitment scale
  - Asserts: won't speak
  - Implicates: certainly won't help

- "I **don't have a dollar, let alone ten dollars**"
  - P: have $1 (smaller amount)
  - Q: have $10 (larger amount)
  - Presupposes: $10 > $1
  - Asserts: don't have $1
  - Implicates: certainly don't have $10

**MUCH LESS / STILL LESS** (Formal, negative polarity):

Synonymous with "let alone" but more formal

- "He **can't afford a bicycle, much less a car**"
  - P: bicycle (cheaper)
  - Q: car (more expensive)
  - Formal register

- "She **won't agree to a delay, still less a cancellation**"
  - P: delay (temporary)
  - Q: cancellation (permanent)
  - Very formal register

**NOT TO MENTION** (Positive or negative, additional emphasis):

- "The plan is risky, **not to mention expensive**"
  - P: risky (first concern)
  - Q: expensive (additional concern often more serious)
  - Can be positive or negative context

- "He's talented, **not to mention experienced**"
  - P: talented (natural ability)
  - Q: experienced (acquired skill)
  - Adds to positive assessment

**A Fortiori Reasoning** (Logical structure):

The logical principle: If Q (more extreme) is true, then P (less extreme) is certainly true

**Positive direction**:
- "If he can lift 200 lbs, he can certainly lift 100 lbs"
- "Not only can he lift 100 lbs, but he can lift 200 lbs"

**Negative direction**:
- "If he can't lift 100 lbs, he certainly can't lift 200 lbs"
- "He can't lift 100 lbs, let alone 200 lbs"

**Negative Polarity Requirement**:

"Let alone" requires **downward-entailing** environment:
- ‚úÖ Negation: "can't walk, let alone run"
- ‚úÖ Questions: "Can you walk, let alone run?"
- ‚úÖ Conditionals: "If you can't walk, let alone run..."
- ‚ùå Positive assertion: *"He walks, let alone runs" (ungrammatical)

**Common Exam Traps**:

- ‚ùå Using "let alone" in positive context:
  - *"He walks, let alone runs"* ‚ùå
  - ‚úÖ "He can't walk, let alone run"
  - ‚úÖ "He not only walks but runs"

- ‚ùå Wrong scalar ordering:
  - *"He can't run, let alone walk"* ‚ùå (walk is easier, wrong order!)
  - ‚úÖ "He can't walk, let alone run" (correct: walk < run)

- ‚ùå Parallel structure errors:
  - *"Not only he won but broke record"* ‚ùå
  - ‚úÖ "Not only did he win, but he broke the record" (parallel aux inversion)
  - ‚úÖ "He not only won but also broke the record" (parallel verbs)

- ‚ùå Confusing scalar direction:
  - "Not only P but Q" ‚Üí Q is MORE extreme/impressive (escalation)
  - "P, let alone Q" ‚Üí Q is MORE difficult/extreme (in negative context)

- ‚ùå Missing "also" when needed:
  - "Not only smart but hardworking" ‚úÖ (adjectives, "also" optional)
  - "Not only passed but also won award" ‚úÖ (verbs, "also" helpful for clarity)

**Scalar Ordering Examples** (Understanding the hierarchy):

| Context | Lesser (P) | Greater (Q) | Construction |
|:--------|:-----------|:------------|:-------------|
| Achievement | Pass | Ace | Not only passed but aced |
| Quantity | Some | All | Not just some but all |
| Cost | Expensive | Prohibitive | Not only expensive but prohibitive |
| Difficulty | Hard | Impossible | Not just hard but impossible |
| Action | Try | Succeed | Not only tried but succeeded |
| Negative ability | Walk | Run | Can't walk, let alone run |
| Negative willingness | Look | Buy | Won't look, let alone buy |

   - First: No one else passed
   - Second: He didn't excel

5. ‚ùå **Position errors**:
   - ‚ùå "Mary passed also" ‚Üí ‚úÖ "Mary also passed"
   - ‚ùå "Too Mary passed" ‚Üí ‚úÖ "Mary passed too"
   - ‚ùå "John even didn't try" ‚Üí ‚úÖ "John didn't even try"

6. ‚ùå **Misunderstanding presuppositions**:
   - "Mary also passed" presupposes others passed (not unique)
   - "Only Mary passed" asserts Mary alone passed (unique)
   - These are **opposite** in meaning!

7. ‚ùå **Formality mismatch**:
   - ‚ùå Academic essay: "The data shows X, plus it confirms Y"
   - ‚úÖ "The data shows X. Moreover, it confirms Y"

### **Category 03: Discourse Connectives & Logical Particles**

#### **3.1 Causal & Consequential Connectives**
- **Markers**: therefore, thus, hence, consequently, accordingly, so, as a result
- **Function**: Signal logical or causal consequence
- **Example**: "It rained. Therefore, the match was canceled."
  - At-issue: Match was canceled
  - CI: Cancellation was consequence of rain
- **Formality hierarchy**: hence/thus (formal) > therefore > so (informal)
- **Punctuation**: Semicolon or period before; comma after
- **Logical structure**: Encode inference or entailment relation

#### **3.2 Elaborative & Additive Connectives**
- **Markers**: moreover, furthermore, besides, in addition, what's more, additionally
- **Function**: Add supporting or escalating information
- **Example**: "He's smart. Moreover, he's hardworking."
  - At-issue: He's hardworking
  - CI: This adds to/strengthens previous positive assessment
- **Discourse structuring**: Build cumulative argument
- **Information flow**: Signal continuing same topic/theme
- **Escalation**: Often moves to stronger or more important point

#### **3.3 Explanatory & Justificatory Connectives**
- **Markers**: after all, you see, in fact, actually, indeed, for
- **Function**: Provide justification, evidence, or reasoning
- **Example**: "She must be home; after all, the lights are on."
  - At-issue: Lights are on
  - CI: This is evidence/reason for prior claim
- **Epistemic reasoning**: Support inference from evidence
- **Common ground**: Appeal to shared knowledge or logic
- **Backward-looking**: Justify or explain previous utterance

#### **3.4 Reformulation & Specification Connectives**
- **Markers**: that is, in other words, namely, i.e., viz., to wit, specifically
- **Function**: Rephrase or specify previous content more precisely
- **Example**: "He's a polyglot, i.e., he speaks many languages."
  - At-issue: Speaks many languages
  - CI: This is definition/specification of "polyglot"
- **Precision levels**: From loose paraphrase to exact definition
- **Academic writing**: Common in formal/technical discourse
- **Clarity function**: Ensure understanding through rephrasing

### **Category 04: Evaluative & Attitudinal Markers**

#### **4.1 Speaker-Attitude Adverbials**
- **Markers**: fortunately, unfortunately, luckily, sadly, tragically, happily, regrettably
- **Function**: Express speaker's emotional evaluation of proposition
- **Example**: "Fortunately, no one was injured"
  - At-issue: No one was injured
  - CI: Speaker evaluates this as fortunate
- **Truth-conditional independence**: "It's not fortunate that no one was injured" doesn't deny the fact
- **Scope**: Takes entire proposition in scope, not just VP

#### **4.2 Epistemic Modal Adverbials**
- **Markers**: obviously, clearly, evidently, apparently, presumably, certainly
- **Function**: Indicate speaker's epistemic stance toward proposition
- **Example**: "Obviously, he didn't understand"
  - At-issue: He didn't understand
  - CI: Speaker believes this should be evident to addressee
- **Evidentiality**: Marks strength and source of evidence
- **Pragmatic effect**: Can be face-threatening (implies addressee should know)

#### **4.3 Speaker-Oriented Comment Adverbials**
- **Markers**: frankly, honestly, truthfully, candidly, strictly speaking, literally
- **Function**: Comment on manner of speaking or precision
- **Example**: "Frankly, I don't care"
  - At-issue: I don't care
  - CI: I'm being frank/direct
- **Speech-act modification**: Modifies illocutionary force, not propositional content
- **Sincerity markers**: Signal degree of candor or precision

#### **4.4 Surprise & Counter-Expectational Markers**
- **Markers**: surprisingly, oddly, unexpectedly, strangely, remarkably, curiously
- **Function**: Mark violation of expectations
- **Example**: "Surprisingly, he agreed"
  - At-issue: He agreed
  - CI: Speaker didn't expect agreement
- **Mirativity**: Expresses speaker's surprise or newsworthiness
- **Presupposition**: Something about context made outcome unexpected

### **Category 05: Temporal & Aspectual Markers**

#### **5.1 Temporal Expectation Adverbs**
- **Markers**: already, still, yet, anymore, no longer, not yet
- **Function**: Signal temporal expectations and changes
- **Examples**:
  - "He's already left" ‚Üí CI: Earlier than expected
  - "She's still here" ‚Üí CI: Unexpectedly late/long
  - "Haven't you finished yet?" ‚Üí CI: Expected completion by now
  - "He doesn't live here anymore" ‚Üí CI: He lived here before
- **Presuppositions**: Encode temporal frames and expectations
- **Polarity**: "Yet" and "anymore" require non-affirmative contexts

#### **5.2 Repetitive & Iterative Markers**
- **Markers**: again, once more, once again, anew, afresh, re- (prefix)
- **Function**: Indicate repetition of event or state
- **Example**: "She called again"
  - At-issue: She called
  - Presupposition: She called before
- **Iterativity**: Marks multiple occurrences of event type
- **Restitutive vs. repetitive**: "The door is open again"
  - Repetitive: Door was opened before
  - Restitutive: Door was open before (might have been naturally open)

#### **5.3 Change-of-State & Achievement Markers**
- **Markers**: now, then, finally, at last, eventually, in the end
- **Function**: Mark transitions or culminations
- **Example**: "Finally, he arrived"
  - At-issue: He arrived
  - CI: There was delay or difficulty; speaker felt wait was long
- **Achievement readings**: Mark completion after process or delay
- **Discourse function**: Signal topic/temporal shifts
- **Emotional loading**: "Finally" and "at last" often express relief or impatience

### **Category 06: Implicative & Aspectual Verbs**

#### **6.1 Difficulty & Success Verbs**
- **Markers**: manage to, succeed in, get to, contrive to, pull off
- **Function**: Implicate difficulty overcome or effort required
- **Example**: "She managed to open the door"
  - At-issue: She opened the door
  - CI: It was difficult; success was uncertain
- **Counterfactual alternative**: Failure was possible/likely
- **Contrast**: "She opened the door" (neutral, no difficulty implied)

#### **6.2 Failure & Attempt Verbs**
- **Markers**: fail to, try to, attempt to, struggle to, endeavor to
- **Function**: Implicate effort, intention, or non-achievement
- **Examples**:
  - "He failed to notice" ‚Üí CI: He should have noticed; there was expectation
  - "She tried to open it" ‚Üí CI: She might not have succeeded; effort was made
- **Implicative vs. non-implicative**: "Try" doesn't entail failure, but often implicates it
- **Obligation**: "Fail to" often suggests violated expectation or duty

#### **6.3 Other Implicative Verbs**
- **Markers**: remember to, forget to, bother to, dare to, deign to, condescend to
- **Function**: Implicate prior states, attitudes, or evaluation of action
- **Examples**:
  - "He bothered to reply" ‚Üí CI: Reply required effort speaker deems significant
  - "She forgot to call" ‚Üí Presupposes: She was supposed to call
  - "He didn't dare to speak" ‚Üí CI: Speaking would be risky/bold
- **Two-way implicatives**: Some have clear entailments in both polarities
  - "Remember to X" entails X happened
  - "Forget to X" entails X didn't happen

### **Category 07: Expressives & Attitudinal Language**

#### **7.1 Expressive Adjectives**
- **Markers**: damn, bloody, freaking, goddamn, fucking
- **Function**: Express speaker's emotional intensity without affecting truth conditions
- **Example**: "That damn dog barked all night" (truth conditions = dog barked; CI = speaker's annoyance)
- **Properties**: Non-displaceability, speaker-oriented, multidimensional meaning
- **Test**: Cannot be denied: *"That damn dog barked, but I'm not annoyed" is contradictory

#### **7.2 Epithets & Evaluative Terms**
- **Markers**: bastard, jerk, idiot, genius (ironic), angel, saint
- **Function**: Convey speaker's attitude toward referent
- **Example**: "That bastard stole my idea" (at-issue = X stole; CI = speaker's negative evaluation)
- **Social dimension**: Can encode power relations, in-group/out-group status
- **Reclamation**: Some terms undergo reappropriation by targeted groups

#### **7.3 Diminutives & Augmentatives**
- **Morphological markers**: -let, -ie/-y, -ling, -ette
- **Lexical markers**: little, tiny, great, grand
- **Function**: Express size + affection/condescension
- **Examples**:
  - "My little project" (affection or belittling depending on context)
  - "booklet" (small + sometimes cute/unimportant)
  - "grand plan" (impressive + potentially ironic)
- **Cross-linguistic**: Spanish -ito/-ita, Italian -ino/-ina, German -chen/-lein

#### **7.4 Interjections & Exclamatives**
- **Markers**: alas, hooray, ouch, wow, damn, phew, yikes, ugh
- **Function**: Express immediate emotional reactions
- **Example**: "Alas, he never returned" (at-issue = didn't return; CI = speaker's sorrow)
- **Properties**: Performative, emotionally deictic, often prosodically marked
- **Discourse function**: Can function as standalone speech acts

### **Category 08: Appositives & Supplementary Expressions**

#### **8.1 Non-Restrictive Relative Clauses**
- **Structure**: who/which clauses (comma-bounded)
- **Function**: Add backgrounded information separate from main assertion
- **Example**: "My brother, who lives in Paris, called"
  - At-issue: Brother called
  - CI: Brother lives in Paris (presupposed/backgrounded)
- **Diagnostic**: Projects under negation, questions, modals
- **Contrast with restrictive**: "My brother who lives in Paris called" (restrictive, identifies which brother)

#### **8.2 Nominal Appositives**
- **Structure**: NP, NP‚ÇÇ, VP
- **Function**: Provide additional identification or characterization
- **Example**: "Ed, a monster, terrorized the village"
  - At-issue: Ed terrorized village
  - CI: Ed is a monster
- **Projection**: "Ed, a monster, didn't terrorize the village" (still CI: Ed is a monster)
- **Types**: Identificational, characterizational, metalinguistic

#### **8.3 Parenthetical Insertions**
- **Markers**: I think, I believe, it seems, as you know, if I recall, reportedly
- **Function**: Add speaker commentary, hedge, or evidence marking
- **Example**: "She is, I believe, correct"
  - At-issue: She is correct
  - CI: Speaker believes this
- **Prosody**: Typically marked by comma intonation
- **Epistemic function**: Mitigate commitment, show evidential source

### **Category 09: Evidential & Modal Markers**

#### **9.1 Evidential Markers**
- **Markers**: reportedly, allegedly, apparently, seemingly, supposedly, evidently
- **Function**: Indicate information source and epistemic stance
- **Examples**:
  - "Reportedly, he resigned" ‚Üí CI: I heard this from others; I'm not committed to truth
  - "Allegedly, she stole it" ‚Üí CI: Someone accuses her; I remain neutral
  - "Apparently, they're dating" ‚Üí CI: There's evidence suggesting this; not confirmed
- **Direct vs. indirect evidence**: Languages vary in grammatical marking
- **Epistemic commitment**: Ranges from hearsay (low) to inference (medium)
- **Legal/journalistic register**: Hedging tool for unverified claims

#### **9.2 Epistemic Certainty Particles**
- **Markers**: surely, certainly, definitely, undoubtedly, indeed, of course
- **Function**: Express high speaker confidence, often appealing to common ground
- **Example**: "Surely you remember our meeting"
  - At-issue: You remember
  - CI: Speaker believes this should be obvious/shared knowledge
- **Rhetorical force**: Can pressure addressee to agree
- **Common ground appeal**: "Of course" presupposes shared knowledge
- **Politeness effects**: Can be face-threatening if wrong

#### **9.3 Epistemic Uncertainty Particles**
- **Markers**: perhaps, maybe, possibly, conceivably, presumably, probably
- **Function**: Express speaker uncertainty, hedge commitments
- **Example**: "Perhaps he's right"
  - At-issue: He might be right
  - CI: Speaker is uncertain, not committed
- **Probability scales**: From "conceivably" (low) to "probably" (high)
- **Hedging strategies**: Soften face-threatening acts, show epistemic humility
- **Indirect speech acts**: "Perhaps you could help?" (polite request)

### **Category 10: Metalinguistic & Repair Markers**

#### **10.1 Reformulation & Self-Correction**
- **Markers**: I mean, that is to say, or rather, better yet, or better
- **Function**: Self-correction or clarification in ongoing discourse
- **Example**: "He's tall‚ÄîI mean, very tall."
  - At-issue: He's very tall
  - CI: Speaker corrects/refines previous statement
- **Online processing**: Marks repair in real-time production
- **Discourse management**: Show speaker monitoring own speech
- **Metalinguistic awareness**: Comment on language use itself

#### **10.2 Hedge & Approximation Markers**
- **Markers**: kind of, sort of, approximately, roughly, more or less, around, about
- **Function**: Signal imprecision, tentativeness, or category approximation
- **Examples**:
  - "It's kind of expensive" ‚Üí CI: Not exactly expensive, but close
  - "There were approximately 50 people" ‚Üí CI: Close to 50, not exact
- **Vagueness**: Deliberately imprecise for various reasons (lack of info, politeness)
- **Politeness strategies**: Soften negative evaluations
- **Generation marker**: "Kind of/sort of" especially common in younger speakers

### **Category 11: Presupposition-Heavy Constructions**

#### **11.1 Factive Predicates**
- **Markers**: know, realize, regret, be aware, discover, find out, notice, remember
- **Function**: Presuppose truth of complement clause
- **Example**: "She regrets leaving early"
  - Assertion: She has regretful feelings
  - Presupposition: She did leave early
- **Projection test**: "She doesn't regret leaving early" (still presupposes she left)
- **Family of factives**:
  - Cognitive: know, realize, be aware, learn, discover
  - Emotive: regret, be sorry, be happy/sad that
  - Perceptual: see that, notice, observe
- **Theoretical status**: Debate whether presuppositions are CIs or separate phenomenon

#### **11.2 Change-of-State Verbs**
- **Markers**: stop, start, begin, continue, cease, resume, quit, keep
- **Function**: Presuppose prior or current states
- **Examples**:
  - "He stopped smoking" ‚Üí Presupposes: He smoked before
  - "She continued working" ‚Üí Presupposes: She was working before
  - "They started arguing" ‚Üí Presupposes: They weren't arguing before
- **Aspectual presuppositions**: Encode temporal transitions
- **Test**: Negation preserves presupposition
  - "He didn't stop smoking" ‚Üí Still presupposes he smoked

#### **11.3 Cleft Constructions**
- **Types**: It-clefts, wh-clefts (pseudo-clefts), reverse wh-clefts
- **Function**: Presuppose background, assert focus
- **Examples**:
  - It-cleft: "It was JOHN who left early"
    - Presupposition: Someone left early
    - Assertion: That person was John
  - Wh-cleft: "What John did was leave early"
    - Presupposition: John did something
    - Assertion: He left early
- **Exhaustiveness**: Often implicates no one else (exclusive identification)
- **Information structure**: Marks given/new information distribution
- **Contrastive focus**: Often corrects or contrasts with prior assumptions

#### **11.4 Definite Descriptions**
- **Markers**: the X, possessives (John's Y), demonstratives (that X)
- **Function**: Presuppose existence and uniqueness
- **Example**: "The king of France is bald"
  - Presupposition: There exists a unique king of France
  - (Classic example‚Äîpresupposition fails!)
- **Russell vs. Strawson debate**:
  - Russell: False (no king exists)
  - Strawson: Neither true nor false (presupposition failure)
- **Accommodation**: Listeners can add presupposed information to common ground
- **Bridging**: "I went to a house. The door was red" (door presupposed by house)

### **Category 14: Theoretical Foundations & Advanced Topics**

#### **14.1 The Projection Problem**
- **Question**: How do CIs behave under operators (negation, questions, modals, conditionals)?
- **Key property**: CIs typically **project** (survive embedding)
- **Examples**:
  - "John didn't manage to open it" ‚Üí Still implies it was difficult
  - "Did that bastard steal it?" ‚Üí Still implies speaker's negative attitude
  - "If John's a bastard, he deserves it" ‚Üí CI can be filtered
- **Comparison with presuppositions**: Similar projection behavior
- **Plugs and filters**: Some contexts block projection
  - Plugs: "say that", "claim that" can block
  - Filters: Conditionals can filter in antecedent position

#### **14.2 Multidimensional Semantics**
- **Core idea**: CIs operate on separate semantic dimension from at-issue content
- **Potts (2005)**: Logic of Conventional Implicatures (LCI)
  - At-issue tier: Truth-conditional content
  - CI tier: Non-truth-conditional, expressive content
- **Compositionality**: How do CIs combine with at-issue content?
- **Independence**: CI content is scopeless, speaker-oriented
- **Formal frameworks**:
  - Type-shifting: CI-bearing expressions have special types
  - Separate dimensions: Parallel semantic computation
  - Application parsetree: Mixed types compose via application

#### **14.3 CI vs. Presupposition vs. Conversational Implicature**

**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**

| Property | CI | Presupposition | Conversational Implicature |
|----------|-----|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Cancellability** | No | No | Yes |
| **Detachability** | Yes (lexically bound) | Yes (lexically bound) | No (content-based) |
| **Calculability** | No (conventional) | No (conventional) | Yes (from maxims) |
| **Projection** | Yes | Yes | No |
| **Speaker-orientation** | Yes | No | Sometimes |
| **Truth-conditional** | No | No (but affects felicity) | No |

**Diagnostic Tests:**

1. **Hey-wait-a-minute test** (Presupposition):
   - A: "John stopped smoking."
   - B: "Hey wait a minute, I didn't know he smoked!"
   - Tests whether content is presupposed (backgrounded)

2. **Cancellability test** (Conversational Implicature):
   - "Some students passed; in fact, all of them did." ‚úì
   - "That damn dog barked; #but I'm not annoyed." ‚úó

3. **Projection test** (CI & Presupposition):
   - Embed under negation, questions, modals‚Äîdoes it survive?

4. **Detachability test** (CI & Presupposition):
   - Can you say the same at-issue content without the implicature by changing words?
   - "But" vs. "and": Same logical content, different CI

**Boundary cases & debates:**
- Are scalar implicatures ("some" ‚Üí "not all") CIs or conversational?
- Are all presuppositions CIs? (No consensus)
- "Even": CI or presupposition? (Shows overlap in properties)

#### **14.4 Cross-Linguistic Patterns**
- **How languages encode CIs**:
  - Morphological: Affixes, particles (Japanese honorifics, German modal particles)
  - Syntactic: Word order, clause structure (appositives)
  - Lexical: Dedicated words (but, even, damn)
  - Prosodic: Intonation, stress patterns
- **Typological variation**:
  - Evidential systems: Some languages grammaticize evidentiality extensively (Turkish, Quechua)
  - Honorific systems: Varying complexity (Japanese > Korean > European languages)
  - Modal particles: Rich in German, less developed in English
- **Universals**: All languages have some CI-like meanings, but encoding strategies vary

#### **14.5 Acquisition & Processing**
- **L1 Acquisition**: Children acquire CIs later than basic semantics
  - "Only" and "even" acquired later than logical operators
  - Presuppositions emerge gradually through childhood
  - Social CIs (honorifics, slang) tied to socialization
- **Processing costs**: CIs can slow processing
  - Appositives processed separately from main content
  - Expressives trigger emotional processing
  - Presuppositions must be accommodated if not in common ground
- **Experimental findings**:
  - ERP studies: Different neural signatures for at-issue vs. CI
  - Eye-tracking: Readers pause longer at presupposition failures
  - Truth-value judgment tasks: CIs don't affect truth judgments

### **Category 15: Practice & Application**

#### **15.1 Key Diagnostic Tests**

**A. Projection Test**
- Embed in negation, questions, modals, conditionals
- If meaning survives ‚Üí CI or presupposition
- Example: "That bastard didn't steal it" ‚Üí Negative attitude survives

**B. Cancellability Test**
- Try to explicitly cancel
- If cancellable ‚Üí conversational implicature
- If contradictory ‚Üí CI or presupposition

**C. Detachability Test**
- Express same at-issue content with different words
- If implicature changes ‚Üí detachable (likely CI)
- If implicature persists ‚Üí not detachable (likely conversational)

**D. Hey-Wait-A-Minute Test**
- Can addressee challenge background content?
- If yes ‚Üí presupposition

**E. Speaker-Orientation Test**
- Is content tied to speaker's attitude/perspective?
- If yes ‚Üí likely CI (especially expressives)

## üéØ Common Properties Across All Types

### **Non-Cancellability**
All conventional implicatures are **non-cancellable**:
- ‚ùå *John is poor but happy, but I don't mean to imply contrast.* (contradictory)
- ‚ùå *Only John passed, but others passed too.* (contradictory)
- ‚ùå *Luckily, he survived, but I don't think it's lucky.* (contradictory)

### **Context-Independence**
Meaning is **lexically fixed**, not derived from context:
- "But" always signals contrast (not context-dependent)
- "Only" always restricts (not cancellable by context)
- "Luckily" always conveys positive evaluation (not negotiable)

### **Truth-Preserving**
Conventional implicatures **don't affect truth conditions**:
- *John is poor but happy.* ‚Üí Truth of "John is happy" unchanged by "but"
- *Only John passed.* ‚Üí Truth of "John passed" unchanged by "only"
- *Luckily, the train arrived on time.* ‚Üí Truth of arrival unchanged by "luckily"

## üìä Cross-Type Comparisons

### **Contrastive vs. Concessive**
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Both** signal unexpected relationships
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Contrastive**: Emphasizes opposition (*John is poor but happy.*)
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ **Concessive**: Emphasizes obstacle-override (*It rained. Still, they went out.*)

### **Additive vs. Focus (Also vs. Only)**
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Additive (also)**: Adds to set; presupposes others (*John also passed.*)
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ **Focus (only)**: Restricts to minimal set; excludes others (*Only John passed.*)

### **Conclusive vs. Additive (Therefore vs. And)**
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Conclusive (therefore)**: Logical inference (*It rained. Therefore, match canceled.*)
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ **Additive (and)**: Simple addition (*It rained, and it was cold.*)

### **Evaluative vs. Propositional Content**
‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ **Evaluative**: Speaker's attitude (*Luckily, he arrived.*)
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ **Propositional**: Objective fact (*He arrived.*)

### What to Avoid

| ‚úÖ DO | ‚ùå DON'T |
|:------|:---------|
| Place focus particles immediately before focused element | Use "also" and "too" together (redundant) |
