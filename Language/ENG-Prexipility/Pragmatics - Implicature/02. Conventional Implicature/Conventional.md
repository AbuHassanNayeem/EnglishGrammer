Conventional Implicature
├── **Definition**
│   ├── Meaning fixed by lexical items, independent of context.
│   ├── Adds extra meaning without affecting the truth value of the sentence.
│   ├── Proposed by Grice; distinct from conversational implicature.
│   ├── Independent of cooperative principles; arises from lexical/conventional properties.
│   └── Contrasts with conversational implicature (context-dependent and cancellable).
│
├── **Key Properties**
│   ├── **Not cancellable**
│   │   ├── Cannot be negated without contradiction.
│   │   ├── Example: *John is poor but happy.* → "but" implies contrast; cannot cancel.
│   │   └── Exam Tip: MCQs often test non-cancellability.
│   │
│   ├── **Context-independent**
│   │   ├── Meaning holds regardless of conversational context.
│   │   └── Example: *Even John passed the exam.* → "even" conveys unexpectedness intrinsically.
│   │
│   └── **Lexically triggered**
│       ├── Specific words/particles trigger the implicature.
│       ├── Common triggers: *but, even, yet, moreover, still, too, also, therefore, consequently, thus, hence, only, indeed, luckily, obviously*.
│       ├── Example: *She is talented, yet humble.* → "yet" triggers contrast.
│       └── Exam Tip: Memorize typical lexical triggers; often asked in SSC/BCS/IELTS.
│
├── **Core Function / Role in Sentence**
│   ├── Provides attitudinal, contrastive, evaluative, or inferential meaning.
│   ├── Signals speaker's perspective or logical connections.
│   ├── Adds additional meaning without altering truth value of main clause.
│   ├── Conveys attitude, emphasis, contrast, or evaluation.
│   ├── Works in parallel with entailment; does not affect truth.
│   ├── Maintains truth value of main clause.
│   └── Supports nuance, clarity, logical inference in formal and informal registers.
│
├── **Function / Role in Sentence**
│   ├── Adds **additional meaning** without altering truth value of main clause.
│   ├── Conveys **attitude, emphasis, contrast, or evaluation**.
│   ├── Works **in parallel with entailment**; does not affect truth.
│   └── Example: *John is poor but happy.* → Truth of “John is happy” remains; “but” adds conventional contrast.
│
├── **Main Types / Lexical Triggers**
│   ├── **Contrastive Conjunctions**
│   │   ├── **Definition**: Words that indicate contrast between clauses.
│   │   ├── **Function**: Highlight unexpected or opposing relationships; adds contrastive meaning.
│   │   ├── **Examples**
│   │   │   ├── *John is poor but happy.* → "but" signals contrast.
│   │   │   └── *She studied hard, yet failed the exam.* → "yet" signals contrast.
│   │   ├── **Variations / Forms**: *but, yet, although, whereas*.
│   │   └── **Transformations**: Cannot replace with "and" without losing implicature.
│   │
│   ├── **Focus-Sensitive Particles**
│   │   ├── **Definition**: Words that emphasize unexpectedness, restriction, or emphasis.
│   │   ├── **Function**: Signal importance or highlight particular elements.
│   │   ├── **Examples**
│   │   │   ├── *Even John passed the exam.* → emphasizes John's unexpected success.
│   │   │   └── *Only Mary attended the meeting.* → restricts scope.
│   │   ├── **Variations / Forms**: *even, only, just, merely*.
│   │   └── **Transformations**: Cannot be removed or paraphrased without losing meaning.
│   │
│   ├── **Conclusive / Inferential Particles**
│   │   ├── **Definition**: Words that indicate logical consequence or inference.
│   │   ├── **Function**: Signal reasoning or conclusion from prior statement.
│   │   ├── **Examples**
│   │   │   ├── *It rained; therefore, the match was canceled.*
│   │   │   └── *She didn't study; consequently, she failed.*
│   │   ├── **Variations / Forms**: *therefore, consequently, thus, hence* (formal).
│   │   └── **Transformations**: Cannot cancel or ignore; meaning is lexical.
│   │
│   ├── **Additive Particles**
│   │   ├── **Definition**: Words that add information or include additional elements.
│   │   ├── **Function**: Provide additional meaning without affecting clause truth.
│   │   ├── **Examples**
│   │   │   ├── *John is kind and smart.* → "and" adds information.
│   │   │   └── *She speaks French and also German.* → "also" adds to the list.
│   │   ├── **Variations / Forms**: *and, also, too, moreover, furthermore*.
│   │   └── **Transformations**: Cannot remove trigger without losing additive implicature.
│   │
│   ├── **Concessive Particles**
│   │   ├── **Definition**: Words that indicate concession or despite expectations.
│   │   ├── **Function**: Conveys concession, despite expectations. 
│   │   ├── **Examples**: 
│   │   │   ├── *It was raining; still, they went out.*  
│   │   │   └── *She tried hard; nevertheless, she failed.*
│   │   ├── **Variations / Forms**: *still, nevertheless, nonetheless*.
│   │   └── **Transformations**: Cannot cancel or ignore; meaning is lexical.
│   │
│   └── **Evaluative / Attitudinal Particles**
│       ├── **Definition**: Words that express speaker's attitude or evaluation toward the proposition.
│       ├── **Function**: Conveys speaker attitude, emotion, or judgment.  
│       ├── **Examples**: 
│       │   ├── *Luckily, the train arrived on time.* → "luckily" conveys positive evaluation.
│       │   └── *Unfortunately, he missed the bus.* → "unfortunately" conveys negative evaluation.
│       ├── **Variations / Forms**: *indeed, obviously, luckily, unfortunately, frankly*.  
│       └── **Transformations**: Cannot cancel or ignore; meaning is lexical.
│
├── **Trigger Words** (Detailed)
│   ├── **but**: Signals contrast between clauses. Example: *John is poor but happy.*
│   ├── **even**: Emphasizes unexpectedness or surprise. Example: *Even John passed the exam.*
│   ├── **yet**: Conveys contrast or continuation. Example: *She tried hard, yet failed.*
│   └── **therefore**: Signals logical conclusion or inference. Example: *It rained; therefore, the match was canceled.*
│
├── **Transformations / Paraphrasing**
│   ├── Cannot usually be reduced without losing implicature.
│   └── Example: *John is poor but happy.* → Cannot change "but" to "and" without altering meaning.
│   └── Exam Tip: Look for lexical triggers; paraphrasing test often appears in IELTS/TOEFL.  
│
├── **Advanced / Edge Cases**
│   ├── Interaction with Presupposition: CI adds extra meaning without affecting presupposed content.
│   ├── Embedded CI: Can appear in subordinate clauses. Example: *Even if John fails, he will try again.*
│   ├── Multiple CI triggers: Example: *John is smart but lazy, yet hardworking.* or *John is rich but generous, yet modest.*
│   └── Formal vs Informal Usage: *therefore, hence, indeed* (formal); *but* (neutral/universal).
│
├── **Common Mistakes**
│   ├── Confusing with conversational implicature (context-dependent).
│   ├── Attempting to cancel CI → incorrect.
│   ├── Misinterpreting lexical triggers as truth-conditional.
│   └── Overlooking multiple triggers in one sentence.
│
├── **Comparison with Similar Structures**
│   ├── **Conversational Implicature**: Context-dependent, cancellable; can be overridden.
│   ├── **Presupposition**: Background assumption, survives negation; CI adds extra meaning but is not presupposed.
│   └── **Entailment**: Truth-preserving, non-cancellable; CI adds meaning without changing truth value.
│
└── **Quick Revision Rules**
    ├── Conventional implicature = fixed meaning, not context.
    ├── Non-cancellable; lexical triggers define meaning.
    ├── Typical triggers: *but, yet, even, only, therefore, also, still, indeed*.
    ├── Adds contrast, emphasis, evaluation, or logical conclusion; truth unchanged.
    ├── Lexical, fixed, non-cancellable, context-independent.
    ├── Cannot cancel or paraphrase without losing meaning.
    └── Differentiate from conversational implicature, presupposition, and entailment in MCQs/exams (SSC/BCS/IELTS/TOEFL).





**GRAMMAR TOPIC: Conventional Implicatures (Lexical Triggers)**  
│  
├── **Definition**: Conventional implicatures arise from fixed lexical expressions carrying non-literal meanings that cannot be cancelled without contradiction, distinct from cancellable conversational implicatures.  
│  
├── **Functions/Purpose**:  
│ ├── Signals indirect speech acts (requests, loaded questions)  
│ ├── Adds scalar or emphatic semantics beyond literal reading  
│ └── Drives discourse inference in reading/listening comprehension  
│  
├── **Structure/Types**:  
│ ├── **Type 1: Speech Act Triggers**  
│ │ ├── **Pattern**: *Aux + NP* → Indirect request/loaded Q  
│ │ ├── **Examples**:  
│ │ │ ├── *Can you* pass the salt? → Polite request  
│ │ │ ├── *Have you* stopped beating your wife? → Loaded trap Q  
│ │ │ └── *Could you* open the window? → Request (ability Q)  
│ │ └── **Exam Tip**: TOEFL traps literal "yes" answers (IELTS Speaking)  
│ ├── **Type 2: Scalar/Emphatic Triggers**  
│ │ ├── **Pattern**: *Even/Only + NP* → Surprise/minimal scalar  
│ │ ├── **Examples**:  
│ │ │ ├── *Even I* know that. → Surprisingly easy  
│ │ │ ├── *Only John* passed. → Minimal set (scalar excl.)  
│ │ │ └── *Even she* failed. → Extreme scalar implicature  
│ │ └── **Exam Tip**: GRE ignores scalar for literal (verb agreement trap)  
│ └── **Advanced: Contrastive Pairs**  
│     ├── **Pattern**: *Not only X but Y* → Escalation  
│     ├── **Examples**:  
│     │ ├── *Not only did he win, but broke the record.* → Stronger claim  
│     │ └── *Not just smart, *he's brilliant.* → Scalar upgrade  
│     └── **Exam Tip**: Punctuation (comma) fails BCS/UPSC sentence correction  
│  
├── **Key Patterns & Word Order**:  
│ ├── Fronted Aux (*Can you*) inverts for indirectness: *Can you help?* ≠ literal ability  
│ └── Scalar *Even NP* precedes VP: ***Even I know even that*** → Word order error (TOEFL)  
│  
├── **Common Exam Traps** (Memorize these!):  
│ ├── ❌ *Can you pass salt? Yes, I can.* → Ignores request → ✅ *Sure, here you go.*  
│ ├── ❌ *Even I don't know.* → Double neg. scalar cancel → ✅ *Even I know.* (non-cancellable)  
│ └── ❌ *Have you stop beating?* → Tense error → ✅ *Have you stopped?* (perfect aspect)  
│  
└── **Quick Revision Mnemonics**:  
    ├── **CALE**: *C*an/*A*ux + Lexical → *E*mphatic scalar implicature  
    └── Fixed phrases *imply forever*—can't cancel without contradiction!

**GRAMMAR TOPIC: Scalar/Emphatic Triggers**  
│  
├── **Definition**: Scalar/emphatic triggers like *even* and *only* add non-literal scalar implicatures signaling surprise, exclusivity, or extremity on an implied scale, non-cancellable in context.  
│  
├── **Functions/Purpose**:  
│ ├── Creates scalar inference (e.g., "surprisingly low/high on scale")  
│ ├── Emphasizes exclusivity or minimal/maximal sets  
│ └── Signals discourse surprise for rhetorical effect (IELTS/TOEFL reading)  
│  
├── **Structure/Types**:  
│ ├── **Type 1: Even (Surprise/Extreme Scalar)**  
│ │ ├── **Pattern**: *Even + NP* → "Surprisingly X does Y" (low expectation)  
│ │ ├── **Examples**:  
│ │ │ ├── *Even I* know that. → Surprisingly easy (low scalar)  
│ │ │ ├── *Even she* failed. → Extreme/low expectation  
│ │ │ └── *Even babies* cry less here. → Scalar surprise  
│ │ └── **Exam Tip**: TOEFL traps literal "all know" → ignores scalar low end  
│ ├── **Type 2: Only (Exclusive/Minimal Scalar)**  
│ │ ├── **Pattern**: *Only + NP* → "NP is minimal set doing Y"  
│ │ ├── **Examples**:  
│ │ │ ├── *Only John* passed. → Minimal set (scalar excl.)  
│ │ │ ├── *Only she* survived. → Exclusive scalar  
│ │ │ └── *Only twice* a week. → Minimal frequency scalar  
│ │ └── **Exam Tip**: GRE verb agreement: *Only John pass* ❌ → *passes* ✅  
│ └── **Advanced: Not Only...But Also (Escalation)**  
│     ├── **Pattern**: *Not only NP1 but NP2* → Scalar upgrade  
│     ├── **Examples**:  
│     │ ├── *Not only smart, **but** brilliant.* → Escalates scalar  
│     │ └── *Not only passed, **but** aced.* → Stronger claim  
│     └── **Exam Tip**: BCS comma splice: ❌ *Not only, but* → ✅ comma required  
│  
├── **Key Patterns & Word Order**:  
│ ├── *Even/Only* precedes focused NP: ***I even know*** ❌ → *Even I know* ✅  
│ └── Parallel structure in *not only...but*: *Not only **did** he run **but** jumped* (aux inversion)  
│  
├── **Common Exam Traps** (Memorize these!):  
│ ├── ❌ *Even I knows.* → Subj-verb agr. → ✅ *Even I know.* (GRE quant.)  
│ ├── ❌ *Only John pass.* → Singular verb → ✅ *Only John passes.* (TOEFL)  
│ └── ❌ *Not only smart but he is brilliant.* → Frag. → ✅ *Not only smart, but brilliant.*  
│  
└── **Quick Revision Mnemonics**:  
    ├── **EOS**: *E*ven = extreme surprise, *O*nly = exclusive minimum, *S*calar scale  
    └── *Even low, only few*—triggers imply scalar forever, no literal cancel!

**GRAMMAR TOPIC: Only (Exclusive/Minimal Scalar)**  
│  
├── **Definition**: *Only* triggers exclusive scalar implicature asserting NP as the minimal/maximal set performing the predicate, implying "no one/nothing else does Y" non-cancellably.  
│  
├── **Functions/Purpose**:  
│ ├── Excludes alternatives on scalar (minimal set doing Y)  
│ ├── Focuses NP as exhaustive/unique performer  
│ └── Signals minimal quantity/frequency (IELTS inference traps)  
│  
├── **Structure/Types**:  
│ ├── **Type 1: NP Focus (Exclusive Subject)**  
│ │ ├── **Pattern**: *Only + NP_subject + VP* → "NP alone Vs"  
│ │ ├── **Examples**:  
│ │ │ ├── *Only John* passed. → John minimal set  
│ │ │ ├── *Only she* survived. → Exclusive scalar  
│ │ │ └── *Only they* agreed. → Minimal group  
│ │ └── **Exam Tip**: Verb agreement: *Only John pass* ❌ → *passes* ✅ (GRE)  
│ ├── **Type 2: Adverbial Focus (Minimal Quantity)**  
│ │ ├── **Pattern**: *Only + Adv/Num + NP/VP* → Minimal extent  
│ │ ├── **Examples**:  
│ │ │ ├── *Only twice* a week. → Minimal frequency  
│ │ │ ├── *Only three* errors. → Minimal count scalar  
│ │ │ └── *Only just* enough. → Barely scalar  
│ │ └── **Exam Tip**: TOEFL adverb placement: ***Only passed twice*** ❌  
│ └── **Advanced: VP Focus (Exhaustive Action)**  
│     ├── **Pattern**: *Only + VP-focus* → "That action alone"  
│     ├── **Examples**:  
│     │ ├── *John only *passed. → Didn't excel (not fail/ace)  
│     │ └── *She only *runs.* → Doesn't jump/swim  
│     └── **Exam Tip**: BCS focus shift: comma isolates VP (punctuation trap)  
│  
├── **Key Patterns & Word Order**:  
│ ├── *Only* precedes focused constituent: ***John only passed*** (ambiguous) → *Only John passed* ✅  
│ └── Singular verb always: *Only I/John/they* + *knows/know* (no plural trigger)  
│  
├── **Common Exam Traps** (Memorize these!):  
│ ├── ❌ *Only John pass.* → Agr. → ✅ *Only John passes.* (TOEFL/GRE)  
│ ├── ❌ *Only she know the answer.* → Singular → ✅ *Only she knows.*  
│ └── ❌ *He only runs, not jumps.* → Frag. → ✅ *He only runs.* (implicature)  
│  
└── **Quick Revision Mnemonics**:  
    ├── **O-MIN**: *O*nly marks *MIN*imal exclusive set  
    └── *Only one does Y*—verb stays singular, scalar excludes all else!

**GRAMMAR TOPIC: Only Adverbial Focus (Minimal Quantity)**  
│  
├── **Definition**: *Only* + adverbial/numeral focuses minimal quantity/extent, triggering scalar implicature of "no more than X" (frequency/count), non-cancellable in context.  
│  
├── **Functions/Purpose**:  
│ ├── Limits extent on quantity scale (minimal amount)  
│ ├── Signals "barely/just enough" scalar inference  
│ └── Traps TOEFL/IELTS in adverb placement/misplacement  
│  
├── **Structure/Types**:  
│ ├── **Type 1: Frequency Adverbs**  
│ │ ├── **Pattern**: *Only + Adv_freq + NP* → Minimal occurrences  
│ │ ├── **Examples**:  
│ │ │ ├── *Only twice* a week. → Minimal frequency  
│ │ │ ├── *Only once* a month. → Rare scalar  
│ │ │ └── *Only rarely* visits. → Minimal occasions  
│ │ └── **Exam Tip**: Adverb before NP: ***Only passed twice*** ❌ → *Passed only twice* ✅  
│ ├── **Type 2: Numerical Quantifiers**  
│ │ ├── **Pattern**: *Only + Num + N* → Minimal count  
│ │ ├── **Examples**:  
│ │ │ ├── *Only three* errors. → Minimal count scalar  
│ │ │ ├── *Only five* minutes late. → Bare minimum  
│ │ │ └── *Only 10%* passed. → Minimal proportion  
│ │ └── **Exam Tip**: GRE quant. agreement: *Only three error* ❌ → *errors* ✅  
│ └── **Advanced: Intensifier + Only (Barely Scalar)**  
│     ├── **Pattern**: *Only + just/barely + Adj/NP* → Threshold scalar  
│     ├── **Examples**:  
│     │ ├── *Only just* enough. → Barely scalar  
│     │ └── *Only barely* visible. → Minimal visibility  
│     └── **Exam Tip**: BCS punctuation: ❌ *Only just, enough* → ✅ no comma  
│  
├── **Key Patterns & Word Order**:  
│ ├── *Only* precedes adverbial: *Only twice a week* ≠ ***Twice only a week*** (awkward)  
│ └── Post-VP safe: *He visits only twice* → unambiguous minimal scalar  
│  
├── **Common Exam Traps** (Memorize these!):  
│ ├── ❌ *Only passed twice.* → Misplacement → ✅ *Passed only twice.* (TOEFL)  
│ ├── ❌ *Only three error remain.* → Noun agr. → ✅ *Only three errors remain.*  
│ └── ❌ *She arrived only just, late.* → Comma splice → ✅ *Only just late.*  
│  
└── **Quick Revision Mnemonics**:  
    ├── **O-QUANT**: *O*nly + *QUANT*ity adverb → minimal scalar forever  
    └── *Only before adverbial*—placement traps kill TOEFL scores!

**GRAMMAR TOPIC: Only Numerical Quantifiers (Minimal Count)**  
│  
├── **Definition**: *Only + numeral + noun* asserts minimal cardinality on a quantity scale, implicating "no more than X items" exclusively, triggering non-cancellable scalar exclusion.  
│  
├── **Functions/Purpose**:  
│ ├── Limits set size to exact minimum (scalar boundary)  
│ ├── Excludes higher quantities rhetorically  
│ └── Tests GRE/TOEFL noun agreement and quantifier placement  
│  
├── **Structure/Types**:  
│ ├── **Type 1: Cardinal Numbers**  
│ │ ├── **Pattern**: *Only + Cardinal Num + N* → Minimal set size  
│ │ ├── **Examples**:  
│ │ │ ├── *Only three* errors. → Minimal count scalar  
│ │ │ ├── *Only five* minutes late. → Bare minimum  
│ │ │ └── *Only two* chances left. → Exclusive scalar  
│ │ └── **Exam Tip**: Plural noun: *Only three error* ❌ → *errors* ✅ (GRE quant.)  
│ ├── **Type 2: Percentage/Proportion**  
│ │ ├── **Pattern**: *Only + %/Fraction + N* → Minimal ratio  
│ │ ├── **Examples**:  
│ │ │ ├── *Only 10%* passed. → Minimal proportion  
│ │ │ ├── *Only half* attended. → Scalar minimum  
│ │ │ └── *Only 1/3* approved. → Fractional exclusion  
│ │ └── **Exam Tip**: TOEFL % placement: ***Passed only 10%*** ambiguous  
│ └── **Advanced: Ordinal + Only (Minimal Rank)**  
│     ├── **Pattern**: *Only + Ordinal + N* → Lowest position scalar  
│     ├── **Examples**:  
│     │ ├── *Only third* place. → Minimal ranking  
│     │ └── *Only second* best. → Exclusive low scalar  
│     └── **Exam Tip**: BCS hyphenation: *Only third-place* finish ✅  
│  
├── **Key Patterns & Word Order**:  
│ ├── *Only* precedes numeral strictly: ***Three only errors*** ❌ → *Only three errors* ✅  
│ └── Plural noun required: *Only one/two/three* + *error/errors* (no singular!)  
│  
├── **Common Exam Traps** (Memorize these!):  
│ ├── ❌ *Only three error.* → Noun agr. → ✅ *Only three errors.* (GRE/TOEFL)  
│ ├── ❌ *Only 10 percent pass.* → Verb agr. → ✅ *Only 10 percent passed.*  
│ └── ❌ *She was only five minute late.* → Possessive → ✅ *five minutes late.*  
│  
└── **Quick Revision Mnemonics**:  
    ├── **O-NUM-PL**: *O*nly + *NUM*eral + *PL*ural noun always  
    └── *Only numbers need plural friends*—singular kills GRE quantifiers!

**GRAMMAR TOPIC: Only Percentage/Proportion (Minimal Ratio)**  
│  
├── **Definition**: *Only + %/fraction + noun* signals minimal ratio on proportional scale, implicating "no higher percentage/fraction" exclusively, non-cancellable scalar exclusion.  
│  
├── **Functions/Purpose**:  
│ ├── Limits proportion to bare minimum (scalar threshold)  
│ ├── Excludes larger ratios rhetorically  
│ └── Tests TOEFL/GRE placement and verb agreement with % subjects  
│  
├── **Structure/Types**:  
│ ├── **Type 1: Percentage Expressions**  
│ │ ├── **Pattern**: *Only + % + N/VP* → Minimal percentage  
│ │ ├── **Examples**:  
│ │ │ ├── *Only 10%* passed. → Minimal proportion  
│ │ │ ├── *Only 25%* approved. → Scalar minimum  
│ │ │ └── *Only 5%* survived. → Extreme low ratio  
│ │ └── **Exam Tip**: % + singular verb: *Only 10% pass* ❌ → *passes* ✅ (GRE)  
│ ├── **Type 2: Fractional Quantifiers**  
│ │ ├── **Pattern**: *Only + Fraction + N/VP* → Minimal fraction  
│ │ ├── **Examples**:  
│ │ │ ├── *Only half* attended. → Scalar minimum  
│ │ │ ├── *Only one-third* approved. → Fractional exclusion  
│ │ │ └── *Only a quarter* finished. → Minimal portion  
│ │ └── **Exam Tip**: Hyphen fractions >1/2: *Only one third* ❌ → *one-third* ✅  
│ └── **Advanced: Decimal/Proportion + Only**  
│     ├── **Pattern**: *Only + 0.X/Decimal + N* → Precise minimal  
│     ├── **Examples**:  
│     │ ├── *Only 0.1%* error rate. → Minimal decimal scalar  
│     │ └── *Only .05* survived. → Extreme ratio  
│     └── **Exam Tip**: TOEFL decimal punctuation: *Only 0,1%* ❌ (comma)  
│  
├── **Key Patterns & Word Order**:  
│ ├── *Only* precedes %/fraction: ***10% only passed*** ❌ → *Only 10% passed* ✅  
│ └── %/fraction as singular subject: *Only 10%/half* + *passes/attends* always  
│  
├── **Common Exam Traps** (Memorize these!):  
│ ├── ❌ *Passed only 10%.* → Ambiguous placement → ✅ *Only 10% passed.* (TOEFL)  
│ ├── ❌ *Only 10% pass.* → Verb agr. → ✅ *Only 10% passes.* (GRE quant.)  
│ └── ❌ *Only one third approved.* → Hyphen → ✅ *Only one-third approved.*  
│  
└── **Quick Revision Mnemonics**:
