## 1. What are Verb Moods?
A **Mood** indicates the speaker's attitude toward what they are saying (is it a fact, a command, or a wish?).

### A. Indicative Mood (Facts)
The most common mood used to state facts or ask questions.
*   *He **is** here.*
*   *Do you **know** him?*

### B. Imperative Mood (Commands)
Used for direct commands or requests. The subject is usually Implicit "You".
*   ***Stop!***
*   ***Please sit down.***

### C. Subjunctive Mood (Unreal/Hypothetical)
Used to express wishes, doubts, or conditions contrary to fact.
*   *If I **were** you, I would leave.* (Not "was").
*   *It is essential that he **be** informed.* (Not "is").

---

## 2. Modal Auxiliary Verbs
Modals are special verbs that express **ability, permission, obligation, or possibility**. They do not change form for the subject.

### Common Modals and their Meanings
| Modal | Function | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Can / Could** | Ability / Permission | *I **can** swim.* / ***Could** I go?* |
| **May / Might** | Possibility / Permission | *It **may** rain.* / ***May** I join?* |
| **Must** | Obligation / Necessity | *You **must** study.* |
| **Should** | Advice / Expectation | *You **should** rest.* |
| **Will / Would** | Future / Hypothetical | *I **would** help if I could.* |

---

## 3. Difference between Helping and Modal Verbs
*   **Primary Auxiliaries (Be, Do, Have):** Used to form tenses. (*I am working.*)
*   **Modal Auxiliaries:** Add specialized meaning. (*I must work.*)
