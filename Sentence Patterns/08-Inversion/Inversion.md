## 1. Introduction
**Inversion** is the reversal of the typical Subject-Verb word order. In standard English, the Subject comes first (`S + V`). In inverted sentences, the Verb (or Auxiliary) comes before the Subject (`V + S` or `Aux + S`).

This chapter covers the rules, types, and stylistic uses of inversion.

## 3. Quick Summary Table

| Type | Order | Example | Function |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Locative** | A + V + S | *Down fell the rain.* | Dramatic / Narrative |
| **Existential** | There + V + S | *There is a cat.* | Introduction |
| **Question** | Aux + S + V | *Can you swim?* | Inquiry |
| **Conditional** | Aux + S... | *Should you need help...* | Formal Condition |
| **Negative** | Neg + Aux + S | *Never will I go.* | Strong Emphasis |

---

## 4. Key Rules
*   **Pronouns:** Locative inversion usually does **not** work with pronoun subjects (*Here he comes*, not *Here comes he*).
*   **Auxiliaries:** Negative and Question inversion require an auxiliary verb (*do, be, have, modals*).
