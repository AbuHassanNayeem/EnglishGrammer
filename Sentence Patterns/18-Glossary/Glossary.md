# Glossary of Grammatical Terms

**Adverbial:** A word, phrase, or clause that modifies a verb, adjective, adverb, or whole clause. Provides information about time, place, manner, frequency, reason, purpose, condition, etc. Label: `A`.

**Adverbial Clause:** A dependent clause that functions as an adverbial, modifying the main verb. Example: *When she arrived, we left.* (when = time)

**Adjective Clause (Relative Clause):** A dependent clause that modifies a noun, introduced by relative pronouns (who, which, that, where, when). Example: *The book that I read was excellent.*

**Agreement (Concord):** The matching of grammatical features between words, typically verb agreement with subject in number and person. Example: *The cat sleeps.* (singular)

**Ambitransitive:** A verb that can be used both transitively and intransitively. Example: *He eats.* / *He eats an apple.*

**Antecedent:** The noun or noun phrase to which a pronoun refers. Example: *Maria left her coat.* (Maria = antecedent of "her")

**Aspect:** A grammatical feature indicating how an action is viewed — continuous, habitual, perfect, etc. Example: *She is reading.* (continuous aspect)

**Auxiliary Verb:** A verb that helps the main verb form tenses, aspects, moods, or voices. Common auxiliaries: `be`, `have`, `do`. Example: *She is reading.* (is = auxiliary)

**Case:** The grammatical form of a noun, pronoun, or adjective indicating its function. Example: subject case (I, he) vs object case (me, him).

**Clause:** A group of words containing a subject and predicate (verb). Can be independent (main) or dependent (subordinate).

**Collective Noun:** A noun representing a group of individuals treated as a single unit or as separate members. Example: team, family, group, committee.

**Complement:** A word, phrase, or clause that completes the meaning of a verb. Types: subject complement (describes subject), object complement (describes/renames object). Label: `C`.

**Complex Sentence:** A sentence containing one independent clause and one or more dependent clauses. Example: *She reads books when she has time.*

**Compound Sentence:** A sentence containing two or more independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction. Example: *She reads books, and he watches films.*

**Compound-Complex Sentence:** A sentence with two or more independent clauses and at least one dependent clause.

**Conjunction:** A word that joins words, phrases, or clauses. Types: coordinating (and, but, or), subordinating (because, when, if).

**Copular/Linking Verb:** A verb that links a subject to a subject complement. Common: `be`, `become`, `seem`, `appear`, `look`, `feel`. Example: *He is a teacher.*

**Declarative:** A sentence type that makes a statement. Example: *She left.*

**Dependent Clause (Subordinate Clause):** A clause that cannot stand alone as a complete sentence; must be attached to an independent clause. Begins with subordinating conjunction or relative pronoun.

**Direct Object:** The object that directly receives the action of a transitive verb. Answers "what?" or "whom?" Label: `O`. Example: *She reads books.* (books = DO)

**Ditransitive:** A transitive verb that takes two objects — an indirect object and a direct object. Example: *She gave him a pen.* (him = IO, pen = DO)

**Expletive:** A word that occupies the subject position without adding meaning. Examples: `there`, dummy `it`. Example: *There is a cat.* (there = expletive; cat = real subject)

**Fragment:** An incomplete sentence; a dependent clause or phrase punctuated as a sentence. Example: *Because she was tired.* (incomplete)

**Gerund:** A verb form ending in -ing that functions as a noun. Example: *Reading is fun.* (Reading = gerund)

**Imperative:** A sentence type expressing a command. The subject (you) is usually omitted. Example: *Close the door!*

**Independent Clause (Main Clause):** A clause that can stand alone as a complete sentence.

**Indirect Object:** The object that indicates the recipient or beneficiary of the action. Answers "to/for whom?" Label: `IO`. Example: *She gave him a pen.* (him = IO)

**Infinitive:** The base form of a verb, often preceded by "to". Example: *to read*, *to go*. Can function as noun, adjective, or adverbial.

**Interrogative:** A sentence type asking a question. Example: *Are you ready?*

**Intransitive Verb:** A verb that does not require a direct object. Label: `V` (intransitive). Example: *Birds fly.*

**Inversion:** The reversal of typical word order, placing the verb before the subject. Common in questions and with certain adverbials. Example: *Are you ready?* (normal: You are ready.)

**Linking Verb:** See **Copular/Linking Verb**.

**Mood:** A grammatical feature indicating the speaker's attitude toward the action. Types: indicative (statements, questions), imperative (commands), subjunctive (wishes, hypotheticals).

**Modal Verb:** An auxiliary verb expressing necessity, possibility, permission, or ability. Examples: `can`, `could`, `may`, `might`, `must`, `shall`, `should`, `will`, `would`.

**Nominative:** The subject form of pronouns and nouns. Example: `I`, `he`, `she`, `we`. Also called **subjective case**.

**Non-restrictive (Non-defining) Clause:** A relative clause that adds extra information and can be removed without changing core meaning. Set off by commas. Example: *John, who is my friend, is here.*

**Noun Clause:** A dependent clause that functions as a noun — as subject, object, or complement. Example: *I know that she left.* (noun clause as object)

**Number:** A grammatical feature indicating whether a word is singular or plural. Example: cat (singular) vs cats (plural).

**Object:** A noun or noun phrase that receives the action of a transitive verb or is governed by a preposition. Types: direct object, indirect object, object of preposition.

**Object Case:** The grammatical case of pronouns that function as objects. Examples: `me`, `you`, `him`, `her`, `us`, `them`.

**Objective Case:** See **Object Case**.

**Particle:** An adverb or preposition used with a verb in a phrasal verb. Example: *pick up* (up = particle).

**Passive Voice:** A sentence construction in which the object of an active verb becomes the subject and the subject becomes optional (agent). Example: *The book was read by her.* (active: She read the book.)

**Person:** A grammatical feature indicating the relationship between the speaker and the subject. Types: first (I, we), second (you), third (he, she, it, they).

**Phrasal Verb:** A verb combined with one or more particles (adverbs/prepositions) to form an idiomatic phrase. Example: *look up*, *run into*, *give up*.

**Phrase:** A group of related words without a subject and predicate. Examples: noun phrase, verb phrase, prepositional phrase.

**Predicate:** The part of a sentence containing the verb and all words related to it. Example: *She reads books.* (reads books = predicate)

**Predicate Adjective:** An adjective in the predicate that describes the subject via a linking verb. Example: *The sky is blue.* (blue = predicate adjective)

**Predicate Nominative:** A noun or noun phrase in the predicate that renames or identifies the subject via a linking verb. Example: *He is a teacher.* (teacher = predicate nominative)

**Pronoun:** A word that replaces a noun. Types: personal (I, he, she), demonstrative (this, that), relative (who, which), interrogative (who, what).

**Restrictive (Defining) Clause:** A relative clause that identifies or narrows down the noun it modifies. Not set off by commas. Example: *The student who worked hard passed.*

**Run-on Sentence:** Two or more independent clauses joined without proper punctuation or conjunction. Example: *She left the party she felt tired.* (should be: She left the party; she felt tired.)

**Sentence:** A group of words containing an independent clause, expressing a complete thought.

**Simple Sentence:** A sentence containing one independent clause and no dependent clauses. Example: *She reads books.*

**Subject:** The noun or noun phrase about which the verb makes a statement. Typically performs the action or is described. Label: `S`. Example: *She reads books.* (She = subject)

**Subject Complement:** A word, phrase, or clause that completes a linking verb and describes or identifies the subject. Example: *He is a teacher.* (teacher = subject complement)

**Subjective Case:** See **Nominative**.

**Subjunctive Mood:** A mood used for wishes, hypothetical conditions, and formal suggestions. Example: *If I were you...* (contrary to fact)

**Subordinate Clause:** See **Dependent Clause**.

**Subordinating Conjunction:** A conjunction that introduces a dependent clause. Examples: because, when, if, although, while, since.

**Tense:** A grammatical feature indicating the time of an action or state. Types: past, present, future.

**Transitive Verb:** A verb that requires a direct object to complete its meaning. Example: *She reads books.* (reads = transitive; books = object)

**Voice:** A grammatical feature indicating the relationship between the subject and the action. Types: active (subject performs action), passive (subject receives action).

**Wh-word:** A question word or relative pronoun: who, whom, which, what, where, when, why, how.

End of glossary.
